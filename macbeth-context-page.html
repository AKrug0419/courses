Setting the Stage

Before we open the text, we need to understand the world Shakespeare was writing in, writing for, and writing about. Macbeth is not simply a story of murder and madness — it is a play saturated with the political anxieties, gender ideologies, and supernatural beliefs of early seventeenth-century England. This page orients you in that world.

As you work through this module, you will trace three interlocking themes across the whole arc of the play. Keep them in mind as you read this context material — you will start to see their roots here, before Act I even begins.

Historical Context

Shakespeare's World, 1606

Macbeth was written around 1606 and almost certainly first performed for King James I of England — who had also been James VI of Scotland since 1567. This is not a minor detail. Understanding who was in the audience shapes everything about why the play is the way it is.

King James I and the Politics of the Play

James was obsessed with witchcraft. In 1597 he published Daemonologie, a book defending the reality of witches and advocating for their prosecution. He believed himself a target of witch-covens. When Shakespeare wrote three witches into the very opening scenes of Macbeth, he was speaking directly to his king's deepest fears — and his most cherished beliefs.

James was also a direct descendant of the historical Banquo, which explains why Shakespeare transforms Banquo into a noble, innocent figure — and why the witches' prophecy promises Banquo a line of kings. It was royal flattery written in iambic pentameter.

The Gunpowder Plot, 1605

Just one year before Macbeth was performed, a group of Catholic conspirators attempted to blow up the Houses of Parliament and assassinate King James. The Gunpowder Plot sent shockwaves through English society. Questions of treachery, loyalty, and the murder of a king were not abstract — they were raw, recent, and terrifying. Shakespeare's audience would have felt Macbeth's regicide viscerally in a way that is difficult for us to fully recover.

The Divine Right of Kings

In Shakespeare's England, the monarch was believed to rule by divine appointment — to kill a king was not merely murder but a cosmic transgression, an offense against God's order. This theology is embedded in the play's fabric. When Macbeth kills Duncan, he does not just take a man's life; he ruptures the natural order. The unnatural storms, the horses eating each other, the darkness at noon — all of these omens reflect a universe thrown out of alignment.

Historical Timeline

Key Dates to Know

~1040

The Historical Macbeth

The real Macbeth was King of Scotland from 1040–1057. He did kill King Duncan I, but in open battle — not by treachery. His reign was long and reportedly stable. Shakespeare transformed history into tragedy.

1587

Holinshed's Chronicles

Shakespeare's primary source. Holinshed's account fused the historical Macbeth with legends and moral lessons. Shakespeare borrowed the broad strokes but invented the psychological depth.

1597

James VI publishes Daemonologie

James's treatise on witchcraft established the theological framework that would give the Weird Sisters their menace for a Jacobean audience.

1603

James VI becomes James I of England

Shakespeare's company becomes The King's Men. The relationship between playwright and monarch grows intimate — and consequential for what Shakespeare writes.

1605

The Gunpowder Plot

The foiled assassination attempt on the king makes regicide a live, terrifying topic in the playhouses of London.

~1606

Macbeth is written and performed

Likely first performed at Hampton Court for King James I. The shortest of Shakespeare's tragedies — and perhaps his most concentrated.

Contextualizing Theme

Masculinity in Early Modern Scotland

One of Macbeth's most persistent preoccupations is with what it means to be a man. But "masculinity" in Shakespeare's world had a very specific set of meanings — and they are not exactly the same as ours.

Valor, Violence, and Virtue

In the world of the play, masculine identity is almost entirely bound up in military valor. The opening scenes describe Macbeth as a warrior of extraordinary ferocity — he "unseam'd" an enemy "from the nave to th' chops." This is presented as admirable. Courage in battle, willingness to kill, and physical dominance are the markers of a "real man."

But the play will complicate this immediately. Lady Macbeth weaponizes the language of masculinity to goad her husband into murder. Macbeth's hesitation is coded as feminine weakness — and "courage" becomes a tool of manipulation. As you read, ask: who gets to define what a man is in this play?

Was the hope drunk
Wherein you dress'd yourself? hath it slept since?
And wakes it now, to look so green and pale
At what it did so freely? From this time
Such I account thy love. Art thou afeard
To be the same in thine own act and valour
As thou art in desire?

— Lady Macbeth, Act I, Scene 7 (a preview of what's to come)

Contextualizing Theme

Witchcraft, Prophecy & Free Will

The Weird Sisters are not simply dramatic devices — they reflect genuine early modern terror. Witchcraft trials were common across Europe; Scotland alone saw thousands of executions. When the witches appear in Act I, Shakespeare's audience was not watching fantasy. They were watching something they believed was real.

What Did People Believe About Witches?

The prevailing theology held that witches were real agents of the Devil, capable of manipulating the natural world — causing disease, storms, failed crops, and death. Crucially, witches were believed capable of prophecy: not because they could see the future, but because the Devil, as a fallen angel, had knowledge of probable futures and could whisper them to his servants.

This means the witches' prophecies are not necessarily true — they may be lures, half-truths designed to damn Macbeth by encouraging him to act. Whether he would have acted without the prophecy is one of the play's deepest questions.

A Question to Carry Into Your Reading

Do the witches cause Macbeth to commit murder? Or do they simply reveal an ambition that was already there? Is Macbeth a man seduced by fate — or a man who uses fate as an excuse? There is no single correct answer. But the question will drive your reading of every scene they appear in, and it will be central to the essay you write at the end of this module.

Learning Activity

Before You Begin Reading

Discuss Discussion Board · Pre-Reading Reflection

Before reading a word of the play, respond to the following prompt in the Module 3 Discussion Board. There are no wrong answers — this is about capturing your thinking before the text shapes it.

  1. On ambition: Think of a time — in your own life, in current events, or in a story you know — when someone's desire for success or power led them to cross a moral or ethical line. What happened? What drove them? Did they succeed?
  2. On fate vs. choice: If someone told you exactly what your future would be, would that change how you acted? Would knowing a destiny make it more or less likely to come true?
  3. On masculinity: What does our culture currently say a "real man" should be? Where do those messages come from — and do you agree with them?
Your post should be 150–250 words. You will also reply to at least one classmate's post before we begin Act I. This discussion will come back to haunt you — in the best way.
Instructor Note — This pre-reading activity is designed to activate students' prior knowledge and personal frameworks before the text introduces Shakespeare's own. Drawing on students' lived experience with all three themes makes the play immediately relevant for first-year students who may approach it with anxiety.
