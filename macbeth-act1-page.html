Act I — Blood & Prophecy

The witches speak. A warrior listens. A kingdom begins to tremble.

Act I Overview

Reading Guide & Learning Goals

Act I introduces every major force in the play — Macbeth's valor, his ambition, the witches' destabilizing prophecy, and Lady Macbeth's electrifying will. By the end of this act, the decision to kill King Duncan has been made. The scenes below are embedded directly from myShakespeare for easy reading; use the annotation and audio features within each scene as you go.

As you work through each scene, keep your three themes in mind. The theme tags beneath each scene marker show you which themes are most active in that scene.

Before You Read

Return briefly to your pre-reading discussion post. You reflected on ambition, fate, and masculinity from your own experience. Hold those reflections loosely as you read — notice when the play pushes back on your assumptions.

Act I, Scene 1

Act I · Scene 1 The Witches on the Heath

Reading Note — This scene is only twelve lines, but do not rush it. Notice what it does not tell you. We do not know yet who Macbeth is, what the battle is, or why these three figures are meeting. Shakespeare withholds context deliberately. What feeling does that create?

Act I, Scene 1 All Witches
  • 1.1.10Fair is foul, and foul is fair,
  • 1.1.11Hover through the fog and filthy air.
Close Reading This chiasmus — "Fair is foul, and foul is fair" — is the play's thesis in ten syllables. It announces that nothing in this world can be trusted to be what it appears. Macbeth will be called brave and be a murderer. Duncan will trust his host and die for it. Lady Macbeth will appear gracious and harbor violence. Every major deception in the play echoes this line. Underline it. You will come back to it.
Stop & Think

In just twelve lines, what mood has Shakespeare established? List three specific word choices that contribute to it. (You can jot these in the margin or your reading notes — you may use them in the study questions.)

  • 1.2.21Till he unseam'd him from the nave to th' chops,
  • 1.2.22And fix'd his head upon our battlements.
Close Reading · Masculinity Theme Notice the Captain's almost ecstatic admiration here. Macbeth's brutality — splitting a man "from the nave to th' chops" (navel to jaw) and mounting his head — is presented as heroic. The phrase "valour's minion" means valour's servant, its favorite. In this world, extreme violence in service of the king is not just permitted; it is the definition of a good man. Keep this image in mind when Lady Macbeth later questions whether her husband is "man enough" to act. The play is asking: is there a difference between killing in battle and killing a sleeping king?

Act I, Scene 3

Act I · Scene 3 The Prophecy — "All hail, Macbeth, that shalt be King hereafter"

Reading Note — This is the scene that sets the entire tragedy in motion. Read it at least twice: once to follow the action, once to watch Macbeth's internal reaction in his asides. An aside is when a character speaks directly to the audience — other characters on stage cannot hear it. These are the moments when we see what Macbeth is really thinking, beneath what he says aloud.

Act I, Scene 3 · The Prophecy First Witch
  • 1.3.48All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Glamis!
Second Witch
  • 1.3.49All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Cawdor!
Third Witch
  • 1.3.50All hail, Macbeth, that shalt be King hereafter!
Close Reading · Fate vs. Free Will Theme The structure of the prophecy is crucial. The first title (Glamis) Macbeth already holds. The second (Cawdor) he is about to be given — he just doesn't know it yet. The third (King) is what the prophecy promises. When Macbeth learns moments later that he is now Thane of Cawdor, the second prophecy has come true without him doing anything. This is the witches' trap: they have made the prophecies feel inevitable, like facts rather than possibilities. But notice — nothing in the prophecy says how Macbeth becomes king, or when. The gap between "you will be king" and "you must kill the king to get there" is entirely Macbeth's own imagination filling in the blank.
Act I, Scene 3 · Macbeth's First Aside Macbeth
  • 1.3.130This supernatural soliciting
  • 1.3.131Cannot be ill, cannot be good: if ill,
  • 1.3.132Why hath it given me earnest of success,
  • 1.3.133Commencing in a truth? I am Thane of Cawdor:
  • 1.3.134If good, why do I yield to that suggestion
  • 1.3.135Whose horrid image doth unfix my hair
  • 1.3.136And make my seated heart knock at my ribs,
  • 1.3.137Against the use of nature?
Close Reading · The "Horrid Image" This aside is one of the most important passages in the play for the fate vs. free will debate. Macbeth has not been told to kill anyone. Yet here — alone with the audience — he reveals that his mind has already leapt to murder. The "horrid image" that makes his hair stand up and his heart "knock at his ribs" is one he has generated himself. The witches said nothing about killing Duncan. Where is this thought coming from? Is it the witches' influence — or Macbeth's own desire revealing itself?
Stop & Think — Fate vs. Free Will

Reread lines 1.3.134–137. Macbeth says the prophecy cannot be good because it produces a "horrid image" in him. What does the existence of that image tell us about Macbeth's inner life before the witches arrived?

Write 3–5 sentences in your reading notes. You may use this observation in your Act I Study Questions.

Act I, Scenes 4–6

Act I · Scenes 4–6 Duncan Names Malcolm His Heir · Lady Macbeth Receives the Letter

Reading Note — Three scenes, two pivots. In Scene 4, Duncan announces that his son Malcolm will be his heir — not Macbeth. Read Macbeth's brief aside after this announcement carefully. In Scene 5, we meet Lady Macbeth reading her husband's letter. In Scene 6, Duncan arrives at Inverness as a guest. The dramatic irony in Scene 6 — where Duncan praises the castle's pleasant air as he walks toward his death — is something to sit with.

  • 1.5.45That no compunctious visitings of nature
  • 1.5.46Shake my fell purpose.
Close Reading · Masculinity & Gender Theme "Unsex me here" is one of the most discussed phrases in Shakespeare. Lady Macbeth is asking to have her femininity stripped away — to have the qualities associated with women in her culture (compassion, remorse, tenderness) surgically removed so she can act with the violence she associates with men. This speech reveals something crucial about the play's gender logic: in Macbeth's world, cruelty and ambition are considered masculine traits. Lady Macbeth does not reject this framework — she internalizes it. She believes she must become "less of a woman" to act. Consider: what does that say about the world she lives in?

Act I, Scene 7

Act I · Scene 7 "If it were done when 'tis done" — The Decision

Reading Note — This is the hinge of Act I. Macbeth, alone, talks himself toward the murder — and then back from it. Then Lady Macbeth enters and reverses his reversal. Read the opening soliloquy slowly: this is Macbeth at his most intellectually honest. Then watch how Lady Macbeth's argument works. What exactly does she say to change his mind?

Act I, Scene 7 · Macbeth's Soliloquy (opening) Macbeth
  • 1.7.1If it were done when 'tis done, then 'twere well
  • 1.7.2It were done quickly: if th' assassination
  • 1.7.3Could trammel up the consequence, and catch
  • 1.7.4With his surcease success; that but this blow
  • 1.7.5Might be the be-all and the end-all here,
  • 1.7.6But here, upon this bank and shoal of time,
  • 1.7.7We'd jump the life to come.
Close Reading · Unchecked Ambition Macbeth's worry here is not moral — it is practical. He is not saying "this is wrong." He is saying "this might not work." He fears the consequences: reputation, justice, divine punishment. His reasons for hesitating are almost entirely about self-interest. This is worth noting: Macbeth's conscience is real, but it is tangled up with self-preservation in a way that will make it easier for Lady Macbeth to override it. She will reframe the question from "is this right?" to "are you man enough?"

  • 1.7.49Was the hope drunk
  • 1.7.50Wherein you dress'd yourself? hath it slept since?
  • 1.7.51And wakes it now, to look so green and pale
  • 1.7.52At what it did so freely?
  • 1.7.61Art thou afeard
  • 1.7.62To be the same in thine own act and valour
  • 1.7.63As thou art in desire? Wouldst thou have that
  • 1.7.64Which thou esteem'st the ornament of life,
  • 1.7.65And live a coward in thine own esteem,
  • 1.7.66Letting "I dare not" wait upon "I would,"
  • 1.7.67Like the poor cat i' th' adage?
Close Reading · Masculinity Theme Lady Macbeth does not argue that the murder is a good idea. She argues that refusing to commit it makes Macbeth a coward — less of a man. This is a manipulation tactic, and a devastatingly effective one, because it targets exactly the identity that Macbeth's entire social world has built for him. The soldier who "unseamed" an enemy in battle cannot now be afraid of a sleeping old man. Notice that Macbeth's final response — "If we should fail?" — is not a moral objection. He has already been converted. He is asking about logistics.
Stop & Think — All Three Themes

By the end of Scene 7, Macbeth has agreed to murder King Duncan. Consider: at what point in Act I did this become inevitable? Was there a moment where a different choice was genuinely available to him — or has he been moving toward this since the witches first spoke?

There is no single right answer. Write a paragraph in your reading notes arguing for a specific moment. You will likely use this thinking in your Act I Study Questions.

Act I Study Questions

Graded Act I Study Questions — Submit via Canvas Assignment

Answer each question in complete sentences. Responses should demonstrate that you have read carefully and are engaging with specific moments in the text. Each answer should be 100–150 words. Cite line numbers when referring to specific passages.

  1. On the Witches: In Act I, Scene 3, Macbeth has a private aside after receiving the prophecy (lines 1.3.130–142). What does this aside reveal about Macbeth's state of mind — and what does it suggest about the relationship between the witches' words and his own desires?
  2. On Masculinity: Lady Macbeth's argument in Act I, Scene 7 relies on a specific idea of what a man should be. Explain her argument in your own words. Do you think her argument is effective? What does it reveal about the values of this play's world?
  3. On Ambition: Trace Macbeth's decision-making across Act I. At what point does his ambition shift from a passive desire into an active plan? What role, if any, do outside forces (the witches, Lady Macbeth) play in that shift — and what responsibility does Macbeth himself bear?
Due before our next class session. These responses prepare you for the Acts II & III reading — the consequences of the decision made in Act I begin immediately.
