Weeks 9–11 • Module Six

The Literary Research Essay

Module overview — learning activities, assignments, and resources for all three weeks

In this module, you'll write a literary research essay — an academic essay that combines your own close reading of a short story with evidence drawn from published scholarly sources. This is the major essay of the semester, worth 25% of your course grade.

You've already done close literary analysis this semester. What makes a research essay different is the addition of secondary sources: peer-reviewed scholarly articles that you'll locate using the college's academic databases, then integrate into your essay as expert evidence alongside your primary source (the short story). The core skill — building an argument about a piece of literature using specific textual evidence — is the same one you practiced in your first essay. You're expanding it here.

Course Learning Outcomes
  • Apply conventions aligned to varied writing situations
  • Apply critical reading and writing skills
  • Locate and integrate research sources appropriately
By the End of This Module, You'll Be Able To…
  • Use academic databases to locate relevant scholarly articles
  • Integrate primary and secondary sources using MLA documentation
  • Write a full academic research essay with a Works Cited page
Week 9 Mar. 9–15 — Getting Started: Research & Planning

This week introduces the research essay. Monday's class focuses on planning and organizing the essay — how to move from a topic to a thesis to a working outline. Wednesday's class demonstrates how to use the college's academic databases to find the scholarly articles you'll need. Complete the steps below in order as you navigate through the module.

📚 Required Reading — Complete in Order

1
Read: Prewriting & Planning
Covers how to move from your story selection to a workable thesis, how to develop body paragraph topics, and how to use the outline template to organize your research essay before you begin drafting. Prepares you for the Outline Assignment that follows.
2
✏️ Complete: Literary Research Essay Outline Assignment
Due Thursday, March 12 at 11:59 PM • 2-Day Grace Period — A working outline for your research essay: thesis, three body paragraph topics, and a preliminary list of your three secondary sources. Use the outline template provided. See the Outline Assignment page for full details.
3
Read: Primary and Secondary Sources: What's the Difference?
Explains the difference between your primary source (the short story) and secondary sources (scholarly articles), what each is doing in your essay, and how to tell a reliable secondary source from one that won't work.
4
Read: Using Academic Databases
A step-by-step walkthrough of how to use Valencia's databases to find peer-reviewed scholarly articles about your story. Includes tips for refining search terms and evaluating what you find. Read before Wednesday's class demonstration.
5
✏️ Complete: Literary Research Essay Research Assignment
Due Sunday, March 15 at 11:59 PM • 2-Day Grace Period — Locate and document your three secondary sources using the college's databases. Record the bibliographic information and Citation Tool MLA entry for each. See the Research Assignment page for full details.

Week 11 Mar. 23–29 — Drafting, Documenting & Submitting

This week covers everything you need to complete and submit the essay. Begin with the Academic Integrity page before taking the Plagiarism Quiz. The subsequent readings walk you through how to use your sources inside body paragraphs, how to document them correctly using MLA format, and how to build your Works Cited page. Then move through the drafting and revision stage before your final submission. Complete all steps in order.

📚 Required Reading — Complete in Order

6
Read: Academic Integrity: Plagiarism and Its Consequences
Covers the six types of academic plagiarism (with a video), Valencia's Academic Integrity Policy, the two levels of bureaucracy involved in plagiarism allegations (Institutional and Instructional), and the three possible consequence options (Academic Sanction, Disciplinary Sanction, or both). Read this before taking the quiz — you may keep this page open during it.
7
✏️ Complete: Academic Integrity: Plagiarism and Its Consequences Quiz
Due Wednesday, March 25 at 11:59 PM • 2-Day Grace Period — A quiz covering the six types of plagiarism, Institutional vs. Instructional bureaucracy, the course's academic integrity policy, and the difference between Academic and Disciplinary sanctions. You may keep the Academic Integrity page open during the quiz.
8
Read: What Am I Doing with These Sources? • MLA Documentation: Quoting & Paraphrasing • MLA Documentation: The Works Cited
These three pages explain how sources function inside body paragraphs, walk through the 10-step anatomy of a research essay body paragraph, cover quoting and paraphrasing conventions for both primary and secondary sources (including signal phrases, the four MLA scenarios, and true paraphrase vs. find-and-replace plagiarism), and address the six most common Works Cited formatting problems, with a final pre-submission checklist.
9
Read: Drafting and Revising (page coming soon)
Guidance on moving from your completed outline and sources into a full working draft — structuring your introduction and thesis, integrating sources within body paragraphs, and revising for argument strength and coherence.
10
🖊️ Begin: Drafting and Revising Stage
Write and revise your research essay using your completed outline, located sources, and the MLA documentation guidance from Step 8. This is the active drafting stage — no separate submission required here.
11
Read: Editing and Proofreading (page coming soon)
Covers how to move from a revised draft to a polished, submission-ready essay — checking for sentence-level errors, MLA formatting compliance, Works Cited accuracy, and the pre-submission checklist.
12
✏️ Complete: Literary Research Essay — Final Draft
Due Sunday, March 29 at 11:59 PM • 4-Day Grace Period — 1,200–1,500 words, MLA format, three secondary sources, six total secondary source quotes (four direct + two paraphrases), Works Cited with four entries. Worth 25% of your course grade. See the Final Draft Assignment page for full instructions and your three topic options.
