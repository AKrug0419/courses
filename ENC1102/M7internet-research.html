Using the Internet for Academic Research

Search Strategy · Source Evaluation · The SIFT Method

Orientation

You Already Know How to Use the Internet. This Is Different.

You've been navigating the internet your entire life. You know how to find things. So the honest question is: why does a college course need to teach you how to do something you already do every day?

Here's the answer. The internet you use daily was designed to serve your personal needs — finding directions, watching videos, connecting with friends, making purchases. None of those tasks require you to care whether the source is authoritative, accurate, or current. The algorithm rewards content that gets clicks, not content that is true.

Academic research requires something different: verifiability. You need sources whose claims can be traced back to evidence, whose authors have relevant expertise, and whose purpose is to inform rather than to persuade, entertain, or sell. The skills for finding and evaluating those sources don't come automatically from years of casual internet use — because casual internet use never required them.

The Credibility Problem

On the modern web, credible and non-credible sources are visually identical. A well-designed website for a conspiracy theory looks exactly like a well-designed website for a peer-reviewed journal. A fake "interview" with a poet, written by AI and posted on an anonymous blog, is indistinguishable in appearance from a real interview published by the Poetry Foundation. Appearance alone tells you nothing. You need strategies that go deeper.

A Word About Confirmation Bias

Research consistently shows that people are significantly more likely to accept a source as credible when it confirms what they already believe — and more likely to question it when it contradicts their existing views. This means your gut reaction to a source ("this feels right" or "this seems off") is an unreliable guide to its actual credibility. The evaluation strategies in this unit are designed to replace gut reaction with a systematic process.

Your Research Tools

Three Tools, Three Different Purposes

For this essay, you'll be conducting research using both an academic database and the internet. Before we discuss search strategy and source evaluation, it's important to understand what each of your available tools is actually good for — because they are genuinely different, and using the wrong tool for a given task costs you time and leads to worse results.

The Recommended Workflow

For literary criticism: Start with your library database. If you're struggling to find sources, try Google Scholar to identify relevant authors and article titles, then search for those in the database. Don't rely on Google Scholar as your final source.

For interviews with poets: Regular Google is your best tool here — the library databases don't typically index interview content, and the most useful interviews are often hosted by literary organizations, university press sites, and established journals. But you'll need to evaluate carefully (more on this shortly).

✎
Formative Check — Think First, Then Reveal
1You want to find peer-reviewed literary criticism of Gwendolyn Brooks's "the mother." Which tool should you start with, and why?
Answer

Start with a library database — specifically the MLA International Bibliography, Academic Search Complete, or JSTOR. These databases are curated for academic content and allow you to filter for peer-reviewed sources. Library databases give you the highest-quality results with the least need for additional evaluation. Google Scholar is a reasonable backup if the database returns few results, but it should be a stepping stone to the database, not a replacement for it.

2A classmate tells you that Google Scholar is basically the same as a library database — it's all academic, so it's all credible. What's wrong with this claim?
Answer

Google Scholar's index is much broader than a library database and includes content that is not peer-reviewed — predatory journals, vanity publications, conference papers of varying quality, and in some cases, non-academic websites that contain academic-sounding language. Library databases are curated by subject librarians specifically to exclude low-quality material. Google Scholar is a useful tool for exploring and identifying keywords, but treating every result as equally credible is a mistake.

3You're researching Yusef Komunyakaa's "Facing It" and want to find an interview with the poet. Which tool is most appropriate, and what should you type?
Answer

Use regular Google — library databases don't typically index interview content. Search for: Yusef Komunyakaa interview. You can also add refinements like Yusef Komunyakaa interview Poetry Foundation or Yusef Komunyakaa interview "Facing It" to narrow toward interviews that specifically discuss the poem. Look for results from established literary organizations (Poetry Foundation, Academy of American Poets, Paris Review, university websites) — these are typically more reliable than anonymous blog posts or general content sites.

Search Strategy

Searching Smarter, Not Harder

Most people type a natural-language question into a search bar and accept whatever the algorithm surfaces first. This works fine for finding a restaurant or a news story. For academic research, it produces inconsistent results because search engines aren't interpreting your question — they're matching your words. Understanding how to structure those words gives you far more control over what you find.

Boolean Operators

Boolean operators are words that tell a search engine how to relate your search terms to each other. They work in Google, Google Scholar, and most library databases.

AND
Narrow Your Search
Requires both terms to appear. Use AND to make results more specific and relevant.
Brooks AND "the mother" AND imagery
OR
Broaden Your Search
Allows either term. Use OR to include related concepts or alternate terminology.
imagery OR "figurative language" AND Neruda
NOT
Exclude Results
Excludes pages containing a term. Use NOT to filter out irrelevant meanings.
Dickinson AND death NOT "Emily Dickinson" biography

Phrase Searching with Quotation Marks

Putting a phrase inside quotation marks tells the search engine to look for those exact words in that exact order — not scattered across the page separately. This is one of the most useful techniques you can learn.

Example

Searching Anne Sexton Her Kind analysis without quotes will return pages containing those words anywhere on the page — which might include pages about Sexton that never mention "Her Kind" specifically, or pages about other kinds of literary analysis. Searching "Her Kind" AND Sexton AND analysis tells the engine you specifically need those words together, producing much tighter results.

What to Do with Google Scholar Results

When you find a promising result on Google Scholar, don't stop there. Note the author, title, and journal name — then go into your library database and search for that specific article. The database version will give you reliable full-text access and confirm the source's publication details. Think of Google Scholar as a map that shows you what exists; the library database is where you actually pick it up.

✎
Formative Check — Think First, Then Reveal
1You search for Emily Dickinson death poem analysis and get thousands of results, most of which are about Dickinson's biography or poems other than "Because I could not stop for Death." How would you revise this search?
Answer

Use phrase searching and a boolean AND to specify the poem: "Because I could not stop for Death" AND Dickinson AND analysis. The quotation marks force the engine to find that exact poem title, eliminating results about other Dickinson poems. You could also add terms to narrow the literary element you're focusing on: "Because I could not stop for Death" AND Dickinson AND figurative language, or "Because I could not stop for Death" AND Dickinson AND mortality.

2What is the difference between these two searches: (a) Pablo Neruda love poetry and (b) "Sonnet XVII" AND Neruda AND imagery? Which is more appropriate for finding a secondary source for your essay?
Answer

Search (a) will return everything about Neruda's love poetry, from biographical overviews to general surveys to articles about dozens of different poems. Search (b) is targeted: it uses phrase searching to focus on the specific poem, a boolean AND to require both the poem title and the author, and a content keyword to filter toward criticism that addresses the literary element you're writing about. For your essay, search (b) is far more appropriate — it's looking for exactly what you need instead of generating hundreds of tangential results.

3You find a Google Scholar result that looks relevant: "Confession, Persona, and the 'Her Kind' Speaker in Anne Sexton's Early Work," published in Women's Studies, 2011. What should you do next?
Answer

Take the author, title, and journal name and search for that specific article in your library database — not Google Scholar. This accomplishes two things: it confirms the source is real and accurately described (Google Scholar results occasionally contain errors), and it gives you reliable access to the full text through the database's licensed content. If the database doesn't carry Women's Studies, the library's interlibrary loan service can usually get it for you within a day or two.

Source Evaluation

SIFT: A Framework for Evaluating What You Find

Once you've found a potential internet source, you need to evaluate it before using it. The most widely taught approach in academic libraries today is the SIFT method, developed by Mike Caulfield at the University of Washington's Center for an Informed Public. SIFT is based on how professional fact-checkers actually work — which turns out to be quite different from how most students approach source evaluation.

Why Not Just Read It Carefully?

The traditional approach to source evaluation — read the source deeply, look for signs of bias, check the author credentials on the page itself — has a significant flaw: it keeps your attention focused on the source you're trying to evaluate. But a fake or unreliable source can be designed to look credible, and reading it carefully only exposes you to more of its potentially misleading content.

Research comparing professional fact-checkers to students and historians found that fact-checkers were significantly faster and more accurate at identifying unreliable sources — because they did something counterintuitive: they immediately left the page and opened new tabs to investigate the source from the outside. This technique is called lateral reading, and it's at the heart of SIFT.

S
Move One
Stop
Before you do anything else, pause. Notice your emotional reaction to the source. Are you excited because it seems to confirm what you already believe? Skeptical because it contradicts it? Neither reaction is a reliable guide to credibility.
Ask yourself: Do I actually know anything about this source or website? What's my gut reaction telling me — and why shouldn't I trust it without checking?
I
Move Two
Investigate the Source
Before reading the source in depth, open a new tab and find out who published it and why. Search for the website name or author in Google. What do other sources say about this outlet? This is the core of lateral reading — you're investigating the source from the outside.
Don't look for information in the source about itself. Look for what others say about it. Anyone can claim expertise on their own About page.
F
Move Three
Find Better Coverage
If the source makes a specific claim, look for other credible sources that confirm, refute, or add context to it. Don't rely on a single source. Look for coverage from multiple independent outlets. If only one source is making a particular claim, that's a reason to be cautious.
For your essay: if a source claims something specific about a poem, can you verify that claim in at least one other credible source?
T
Move Four
Trace Claims to Their Origin
If the source cites or quotes something, track it back to the original. A quote from a poet may have been taken out of context, paraphrased inaccurately, or simply invented. Following a claim back to its original source tells you whether it has actually been reported the way the author says.
Be especially alert when a source doesn't link to or name its original reference. The absence of a citation is itself information.

Lateral Reading in Practice: A Worked Example

Let's say you've searched Google for literary criticism of Yusef Komunyakaa's "Facing It" and found the following result: a site called LitAnalyst Weekly with an article titled "The Mirror and the Self: Komunyakaa's Use of Reflection in 'Facing It.'" The site looks professional. The article is well-written. Here's how you'd apply SIFT before deciding whether to use it.

Worked Example
Applying SIFT to an Unfamiliar Website
S
Stop
Notice your reaction — and pause

The article looks authoritative. It uses academic language, quotes from the poem, and makes points that seem insightful. Your instinct is to start highlighting passages. Instead, pause. You've never heard of LitAnalyst Weekly, and that matters.

I
Investigate the Source — Laterally
Open a new tab and search for the site

Without reading further on the original page, open a new browser tab and search Google for: LitAnalyst Weekly. Also check whether the author has any presence outside this site — does their name appear in any scholarly database, university affiliation, or known publication?

What you find: The site's domain was registered eight months ago. There's no About page naming editors or institutional affiliation. A Reddit thread asks whether the site is credible and gets no confident answers. The author's name returns no results outside this site.
F
Find Better Coverage
Look for corroboration from known-credible sources

Search Google Scholar and your library database for: "Facing It" AND Komunyakaa AND reflection. See whether established literary journals have published criticism making similar arguments.

What you find: You find two articles in African American Review and The Southern Literary Journal that discuss similar themes — the mirror imagery, the act of seeing. These are from peer-reviewed journals with editorial boards. The LitAnalyst Weekly article makes some of the same points, but there's no way to tell whether it's drawing on the same scholarship or simply summarizing it without attribution.
T
Trace Claims to Their Origin
Check whether citations are real and accurate

The LitAnalyst Weekly article quotes what it calls "critic David Hayes" saying that "the poem enacts a kind of optical violence." Check whether this quote can be traced to a real publication by a real scholar named David Hayes.

What you find: No scholar named David Hayes appears in any literary database with publication on Komunyakaa. The quoted phrase returns no other results online. This is a red flag — the citation may be fabricated.
✓
Decision
What to do with this source

LitAnalyst Weekly fails on every criterion: no institutional affiliation, a very recent domain, an unverifiable author, and at least one citation that appears to be fabricated. Do not use it. The two peer-reviewed articles you found while doing your lateral research are far better options — and you found them precisely because you applied SIFT rather than simply reading the first result that looked useful.

✎
Formative Check — Think First, Then Reveal
1You find a website that looks credible — clean design, academic language, a named author. According to SIFT, what is the first thing you should do, and why isn't "read it carefully" the right answer?
Answer

The first move is Stop — noticing your reaction and not immediately trusting your instinct that the source looks good. The second move, which you should do before reading carefully, is Investigate the Source laterally: open a new tab and search for what others say about this website or author. Reading the source carefully first is a mistake because a well-designed fake source is designed to seem credible when read carefully. You're evaluating it before you've given yourself a chance to be misled by its presentation.

2What is lateral reading, and how is it different from reading the source carefully?
Answer

Lateral reading means leaving the source and opening multiple new tabs to investigate it from the outside — searching for what other credible sources say about the website or author, rather than reading the source itself more deeply. It's the opposite of vertical reading (staying on the page and reading more carefully). Lateral reading reflects how professional fact-checkers actually work: they gain their accuracy not by reading suspicious sources carefully but by establishing the source's context and reputation before engaging with its content.

3You find a website that quotes Pablo Neruda saying "I love you as certain dark things are to be loved, in secret, between the shadow and the soul." The site says this is from Sonnet XVIII and uses it as evidence that Neruda's imagery is deliberately obscure. What SIFT move should you apply here, and what specifically should you check?
Answer

Apply Trace Claims to Their Origin. You should: (1) verify that the quote actually appears in "Sonnet XVIII" as attributed — it does, so the quote itself is real; (2) check whether the attribution to "obscurity" as the poem's intent is an accurate characterization of scholarly interpretation, or just this site's reading. A real quote used out of context or misread is still a misuse of evidence. The SIFT "Trace" move isn't just about fake quotes — it's about ensuring that the claim the source is making with the quote is accurate to what the quote actually says and what scholars have established about the poem.

A Quick Reference

Signals of Credibility — and Their Absence

Even after applying SIFT, it helps to have a mental checklist of specific markers you're looking for. These aren't a replacement for lateral reading — they're what you use to interpret what your lateral investigation surfaces.

✓
Named, Verifiable Author
The author's name appears, and they have a professional presence (university affiliation, publications, or known organizational role) you can verify outside the site.
✓
Known Publisher or Platform
The source comes from an established organization: a university press, a peer-reviewed journal, the Poetry Foundation, the Academy of American Poets, the Paris Review, or similar.
✓
Traceable Citations
When the source cites other scholars or quotes from poems, those citations can be traced to real, verifiable sources. Fabricated citations are a disqualifying red flag.
✓
Clear Purpose
The source exists to inform — not to sell, entertain, advocate, or generate clicks. Literary criticism aims to illuminate a text; it should not feel like an ad, a blog post, or a summary service.
✗
Anonymous or Unverifiable Authorship
No named author, or a name that returns no results outside the site. If you can't verify who wrote it, you can't evaluate whether they have relevant expertise.
✗
Study Guide / Essay Mill Sites
GradeSaver, SparkNotes, StudyMode, Course Hero, Shmoop, Bartleby, and similar sites are not academic sources. Their content is written for students who haven't done the reading — not by scholars for scholarly purposes.
One More Tool: AI Detectors and Source Verification

As AI-generated content becomes increasingly common on the web, a growing number of sources that look like literary criticism are in fact generated by AI — with no human expert behind them and potentially fabricated citations. Before you commit to an internet source, a quick lateral investigation will often reveal whether the site has a history of publishing legitimate scholarship or has recently appeared with AI-generated content. When in doubt, find a source whose author and publisher you can verify. The next page — AI and Academic Research — addresses this challenge in detail.
