Stage One

Prewriting — Already Done

In the traditional writing process, prewriting is the work you do on a topic before you draft an essay — reading, annotating, discussing, forming initial interpretations. For this essay, that stage is already complete.

In the previous module, you completed two discussion assignments that asked you to closely analyze poems: the Figurative Language Discussion and the Poetic Imagery Discussion. Both assignments required you to select a poem, identify and analyze its literary techniques, and articulate your interpretation in writing. That work is your prewriting.

This means you are already ahead: if you write your essay on one of the poems you analyzed in those assignments, you have existing notes, a working interpretation, and specific lines from the poem already in mind. You can move directly into the planning stage.

Choosing a Different Poem

If you choose a poem you have not yet analyzed — one of the six options you didn't work on in the discussion assignments — you will need to complete the prewriting stage yourself before planning. Read the poem carefully multiple times, annotate it, and work through its literary elements before attempting an outline. This will take additional time; plan accordingly.

Stage Two

Planning — Body Paragraph Structure

The planning stage is where you decide what your essay will argue and how it will be organized — before you begin drafting. Even though you are not required to submit a formal outline for this essay, planning before you write is strongly recommended.

At 700 words minimum and four paragraphs (introduction + two body paragraphs + conclusion), the poetry research essay is focused and tightly structured. There is no room for wandering — every sentence in every body paragraph needs to do specific work. The body paragraph structure below shows you exactly what that work is.

No Outline Assignment This Time

Unlike your first two essays, you will not be submitting a formal outline for the poetry research essay. However, you are strongly encouraged to draft one before you begin writing — even informally. Writers who plan first tend to produce more focused, purposeful essays. If you'd like feedback on your outline before you draft, email it to the instructor.

Body Paragraph Structure

Every body paragraph in your research essay should follow this eight-part structure. During the planning stage, leave items ④ and ⑤ (the research source sections) blank — you haven't conducted research yet. You'll add them when you draft.

Body Paragraph Structure — Poetry Research Essay
① Topic Sentence

Your own claim about what specific aspect of the poem — its imagery, figurative language, or both — you are analyzing in this paragraph, and how it connects to your thesis. Your words only. No sources.

② Primary Quote

A brief, specific quote from the poem that directly supports this paragraph's topic. Introduce with a signal phrase naming the poet: In the poem's opening lines, Sexton writes… Cite by line number, not page number: (lines 1–2) or, after first mention of the poet's name, (1–2).

③ Your Analysis

Explain what the quoted lines mean, what literary technique they use, and how they support your topic sentence. Poems and sources never explain themselves — your analysis is the heart of the paragraph.

④ Secondary Source

A quote, paraphrase, or summary from one of your three research sources. Introduce with a signal phrase. Complete during Drafting stage

⑤ Source Analysis

Explain how the scholar's point deepens your argument — not just that they agree, but specifically how and why their insight strengthens this paragraph's claim. Complete during Drafting stage

⑥ Second Primary Quote

A second set of lines from the poem that extends or complicates the paragraph's argument. Introduce with a signal phrase or a transitional phrase. Cite by line number.

⑦ Your Analysis

Explain what these additional lines contribute to your argument. This analysis should advance the paragraph's point beyond what ③ established — not repeat it.

⑧ Closing Sentence

Wrap up the paragraph's argument and gesture toward the next paragraph's topic (or toward the essay's larger conclusion, if this is the final body paragraph). Your words only — no new quotes.

On Line Number Citations

When you quote from a poem in MLA format, you cite by line number — not page number. In your first use of a quote from the poem, include the word "line" or "lines": (lines 1–4). In subsequent citations, the number alone is sufficient: (5–8). See the MLA Documentation: Quoting from a Poem handout for full guidance on multi-line quotes, slash notation, and block quotes.

1
William Shakespeare
"Sonnet 18" (c. 1609)
Figurative Language
Thesis Statement

In "Sonnet 18," William Shakespeare uses an extended simile that compares his beloved to a summer's day — and then strategically dismantles it — alongside the personification of Death as a boastful creature, to argue that poetry possesses a power that summer and time do not: the permanent preservation of beauty.

I
Introduction Paragraph

Introduce the poem, its author, and its form (14-line English sonnet, published 1609). Identify the essay's analytical focus: Shakespeare's use of figurative language — specifically an extended simile that collapses under its own inadequacy, and the personification of Death — to make an argument about poetry's power to immortalize. End with the thesis statement above.

II
Body Paragraph One — The Simile and Its Collapse: Summer as an Inadequate Comparison
① Topic Sentence
In the poem's first eight lines, Shakespeare introduces a comparison between his beloved and a summer's day — then immediately and systematically dismantles it, using the simile's failure to establish that his beloved surpasses anything nature can offer.
② Primary Quote
Introduce: The poem opens by introducing and immediately qualifying its central comparison… "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?
Thou art more lovely and more temperate" (lines 1–2)
③ Analysis
The opening question is rhetorical — it proposes the simile only to begin departing from it at once. "More lovely and more temperate" elevates the beloved above the comparison before the comparison is even established: summer is lovely, but not as lovely; warm, but not reliably so. The simile's very introduction signals its own inadequacy.

⑥ Second Primary Quote
Introduce: Shakespeare pushes the simile's collapse further with a legal metaphor… "Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May,
And summer's lease hath all too short a date" (3–4)
⑦ Analysis
The metaphor of "summer's lease" — a legal term — casts summer as a temporary tenant who doesn't own its own beauty; it holds the warmth and light on loan, and the lease expires. "Rough winds" and "all too short a date" present summer as unreliable and fleeting, precisely the opposite of what the speaker wants to claim for the beloved. By the end of the octave, summer has been disqualified as a comparison, setting up the problem the poem's final six lines must solve.
⑧ Closing Sentence
By collapsing the summer simile before it is fully established, Shakespeare creates a logical need: if nature cannot preserve beauty permanently, something else must — and the poem's final six lines propose that poetry is that something.
III
Body Paragraph Two — Death Personified: Poetry's Answer to Time
① Topic Sentence
In the sonnet's final six lines, Shakespeare personifies Death as a creature capable of boasting — and therefore capable of being silenced — to argue that the poem itself can defeat mortality and preserve the beloved's beauty permanently.
② Primary Quote
Introduce: Shakespeare addresses Death directly in the poem's turn… "Nor shall death brag thou wand'rest in his shade,
When in eternal lines to Time thou grow'st" (lines 11–12)
③ Analysis
Personifying Death as something that "brags" transforms it into a figure with ego, ambition, and appetite — and therefore a figure that can be defeated. If Death can boast, it can also be silenced. "Eternal lines" is a double pun: both the lines of the poem and the lines of lineage or descent, suggesting the beloved survives through the poem's act of recording. The phrase "to Time thou grow'st" frames survival not as an escape from time but as a kind of rootedness within it.
④ Secondary Source
[Quote, paraphrase, or summary from a second research source — to be added during the Drafting stage] Complete During Drafting
⑤ Source Analysis
[Explanation of how the scholar's point deepens this paragraph's argument — to be added during the Drafting stage] Complete During Drafting
⑥ Second Primary Quote
Introduce: The poem's closing couplet makes the argument explicit… "So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see,
So long lives this, and this gives life to thee." (13–14)
⑦ Analysis
The closing couplet is recursive: the poem is both the argument and its own evidence. "This gives life to thee" collapses the distinction between the text and the person it describes — to read the poem is to keep the beloved alive. The personification of Death in lines 11–12 is what makes this claim possible: once Death becomes a figure that brags, it becomes a figure that poetry can answer and outlast. The couplet's conditional form — "so long as men can breathe" — ties the beloved's survival to the poem's readership, making every reader a participant in the act of preservation.
⑧ Closing Sentence
Shakespeare's figurative language ultimately makes the sonnet a self-fulfilling argument: the poem is simultaneously the case for poetry's power and the proof of it.
IV
Conclusion Paragraph

Return to the poem's central figurative strategy and explain its larger significance. Consider: What does Shakespeare ultimately claim about the relationship between poetry, time, and beauty? Is the speaker's confidence in the power of his verse arrogant, sincere, or both? What does the poem suggest about what literature can — and cannot — preserve? How does the sonnet form itself contribute to the argument?

Do not introduce new quotes in the conclusion. Synthesize what your two body paragraphs argued and leave the reader with the essay's broader claim about what Shakespeare's figurative language achieves.

2
Ada Limón
"Instructions on Not Giving Up" (2018)
Poetic Imagery
Thesis Statement

In "Instructions on Not Giving Up," Ada Limón uses two contrasting strands of imagery — the spectacular, almost garish colors of spring blossoms and the quiet, tactile emergence of green leaves — to argue that genuine resilience is not dramatic or conspicuous but patient, physical, and rooted in the ordinary.

I
Introduction Paragraph

Introduce the poem, its author, and its occasion (early spring, the annual return of growth after winter). Identify the essay's analytical focus: the contrast between two image clusters — the blossoms Limón catalogues and immediately dismisses as insufficient, and the leaves whose quiet emergence she finds genuinely meaningful. End with the thesis statement above.

II
Body Paragraph One — The Spectacular and Its Limits: Blossoms as "Baubles and Trinkets"
① Topic Sentence
In the poem's first half, Limón catalogues the conspicuous, almost excessive visual imagery of early spring blossoms — only to dismiss them, through a shift in metaphor, as cheap and insufficient symbols of genuine renewal.
② Primary Quote
Introduce: The poem opens by cataloguing the spring blossoms in almost overwhelming detail… "More than the fuchsia funnels breaking out
of the crabapple tree, more than the neighbor's
almost obscene display of cherry limbs shoving
their cotton candy-colored blossoms to the slate" (lines 1–4)
③ Analysis
The opening "More than" signals that the speaker is building toward a contrast — the blossoms are vivid, but something else matters more. The imagery is visually rich almost to excess: "fuchsia funnels," "cherry limbs shoving," "cotton candy-colored blossoms." The word "obscene" is startling applied to spring flowers; it frames the display as too much, a visual assault. Limón catalogs the blossoms' colors in precise, sensory detail precisely to set them up for rejection: she can see all this spectacle and still find it insufficient.

[Quote, paraphrase, or summary from one of your research sources — to be added during the Drafting stage] Complete During Drafting
⑤ Source Analysis
[Explanation of how the scholar's point deepens this paragraph's argument — to be added during the Drafting stage] Complete During Drafting
⑥ Second Primary Quote
Introduce: Limón's metaphors shift from sensory description to evaluative judgment… "the world's baubles and trinkets, leave
the pavement strewn with the confetti of aftermath" (7–8)
⑦ Analysis
The blossoms become "baubles and trinkets" — cheap decorative objects, pretty but without lasting value. "Confetti of aftermath" is a precise and surprising pivot: confetti signals celebration, but "aftermath" implies the party is already over, the mess remaining. The word "strewn" adds a note of disorder. Limón's imagery frames the blossoms' beauty as pointing toward its own dissolution — and this makes them inadequate symbols for the renewal the poem is actually interested in.
⑧ Closing Sentence
By cataloguing the blossoms' vivid colors and immediately reframing them as trinkets and confetti, Limón establishes that genuine resilience doesn't look like spring's most conspicuous display — it arrives after the spectacle has passed, in something quieter and more durable.
III
Body Paragraph Two — The Patient Leaves: Tactile Imagery and Quiet Resilience
① Topic Sentence
In contrast to the blossoms' vivid color, Limón's imagery of the emerging leaves is tactile, slow, and physical — a different kind of spring arrival that the speaker finds genuinely meaningful precisely because of its ordinariness and its suggestion of quiet endurance.
② Primary Quote
Introduce: The poem's turn arrives when Limón shifts from color to texture… "the leaves come. Patient, plodding, a green skin
growing over whatever winter did to us" (lines 9–10)
③ Analysis
The imagery shifts dramatically from visual to tactile. "Patient, plodding" are words we associate with labor and endurance, not celebration — the leaves don't burst into color but arrive in steady, incremental growth. "A green skin / growing over whatever winter did to us" is the poem's central metaphor: the leaves function like scar tissue, healing the wound of the cold season. The word "skin" is particularly physical and intimate; it makes resilience a biological process rather than an emotional attitude. And "whatever winter did to us" is deliberately vague — the poem invites the reader to fill in their own specific winter.

[Quote, paraphrase, or summary from a second research source — to be added during the Drafting stage] Complete During Drafting
⑤ Source Analysis
[Explanation of how the scholar's point deepens this paragraph's argument — to be added during the Drafting stage] Complete During Drafting
⑥ Second Primary Quote
Introduce: The poem closes with the poem's most precise and surprising image… "a new slick leaf
unfurling like a fist to an open palm, I'll take it all." (13–14)
⑦ Analysis
The simile "like a fist to an open palm" is tactile, precise, and unexpected. A fist unclenching to an open palm suggests the release of tension, but also readiness — an open hand can receive. The "slick" texture of the new leaf is immediately physical, anchoring the poem's conclusion in bodily sensation rather than abstract consolation. The final "I'll take it all" borrows the tree's imagined voice — and the speaker's acceptance is total, including the unnamed hurt and difficulty that "winter did to us." The poem argues that resilience looks like this: a small, ordinary, physical unfolding.
⑧ Closing Sentence
Limón's contrast between the blossoms' spectacular color and the leaves' quiet, tactile emergence argues that genuine resilience is not something performed publicly but something grown slowly — a green skin, a leaf unfurling, a hand opening.
IV
Conclusion Paragraph

Return to the poem's central contrast and explain its larger significance. Consider: What does Limón ultimately claim about the nature of resilience? Is the poem's final gesture — "I'll take it all" — triumphant, resigned, or both? What does the comparison between blossoms and leaves suggest about how we distinguish genuine renewal from mere display? What does it mean that the poem is addressed as "instructions" — to whom, and for what?

Do not introduce new quotes in the conclusion. Synthesize the two body paragraphs' arguments and leave the reader with the essay's broader claim about what Limón's imagery achieves.
