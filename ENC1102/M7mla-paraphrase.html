How to Create
a Paraphrase

How to incorporate a scholar's ideas in your own words: rules, examples, and the most common errors to avoid.

A paraphrase lets you fold a scholar's argument into your own prose without quoting directly. It is one of the most useful moves in research writing, but it is also one of the easiest to do badly. This page walks you through what a paraphrase actually is, when to choose one over a direct quote, the step by step process for building one, the citation rules, three full examples, and the five most common ways students get paraphrasing wrong.

Part One Core Skills

Section One

What Is a Paraphrase?

A paraphrase is when you take a scholar's idea or argument from a secondary source and restate it completely in your own words. You are not quoting the author directly. You are translating their point into your own prose while accurately representing their meaning. The scholar still receives full credit through a signal phrase, a parenthetical citation, and a Works Cited entry, just as they would for a direct quote.

The key word in that definition is completely. A paraphrase is not a light editing job. It is not a matter of replacing a few words with synonyms while keeping the original sentence structure. It requires that you understand the scholar's point well enough to reconstruct it in an entirely new sentence (your sentence) that captures the same idea.

Three things a paraphrase must do

1
Accurately represent the original
The scholar's actual argument must come through. Distorting or oversimplifying what they said is just as problematic as copying their words.
2
Be written entirely in your words
Every word, phrase, and sentence structure must be yours. The only exceptions are proper nouns (names, titles) and highly technical terms that have no real alternatives.
3
Acknowledge the source
A paraphrase without a signal phrase or citation is plagiarism, even though you rewrote the language. The idea still belongs to the scholar.

Section Two

When to Paraphrase vs. Quote Directly

Both direct quotes and paraphrases are legitimate ways to incorporate secondary source evidence, and your essay will use both. The choice between them depends on what you are trying to do with the scholar's material. Understanding when each is appropriate makes your writing more purposeful.

✓ Use a direct quote when…
  • The scholar's specific wording is particularly precise, striking, or memorable.
  • The exact language is itself the subject of analysis.
  • Paraphrasing would require so much explanation that quoting directly is more efficient.
  • The scholar coins or uses a specific term or phrase you want to introduce.
✓ Use a paraphrase when…
  • The scholar's idea matters more than their specific wording.
  • The original passage is longer than what you actually need.
  • The scholar's sentence is complex or technical and would read more clearly in your own words.
  • You want to vary your evidence use, so that not every secondary source moment is a direct quote.
Reminder

Your essay requires six secondary source uses total across three body paragraphs. These can be a combination of direct quotes and paraphrases. The sample essays use roughly four direct quotes and two paraphrases. Both count equally toward your total.

Section Three

How to Build a Paraphrase: A Step by Step Process

A reliable paraphrase does not happen by staring at the original sentence and changing words one at a time. That process almost always produces patchwriting (see Section 6). Instead, follow these six steps to produce a genuinely rewritten version of the scholar's idea.

1
Read the passage carefully until you understand the scholar's point
Do not begin writing until you can summarize the idea in your head without looking at the text. If you cannot do this, you do not yet understand the passage well enough to paraphrase it.
2
Set the original aside; do not look at it while you write
Scroll past it or cover it on the page. Writing from memory forces you to use your own language instead of the scholar's. This single step eliminates most patchwriting problems.
3
Restructure: start from a different point in the argument
If the original sentence moves from A to B, try writing your paraphrase moving from B to A, or entering from a different angle entirely. This ensures your organization is yours, not the scholar's.
4
Keep it shorter than the original
A paraphrase that is as long as the original passage is almost certainly still too close to it. Aim to distill the scholar's point into one well constructed sentence of your own.
5
Re-read your paraphrase alongside the original and check for borrowed language
Your brain naturally gravitates back toward the original phrasing. Re-reading with the source visible helps you catch any words or phrases that crept in without your noticing. If you find them, rewrite those parts. Do not just swap individual words for synonyms.
6
Add your signal phrase, citation, and your own analytical sentence
A paraphrase without a signal phrase is plagiarism. A paraphrase without your own follow up analysis is a wasted opportunity. The paraphrase delivers the scholar's point; your analysis connects that point to your argument.

Section Four

Citation Rules for Paraphrases

Paraphrases follow the same citation rules as direct quotes: the same four patterns based on source format (PDF vs. HTML) and where you place the author's name (signal phrase vs. parenthetical). The only difference between citing a quote and citing a paraphrase is that a paraphrase has no quotation marks.

PDF Source Author's Name in Signal Phrase or in Parenthetical
Pattern A · Author in Signal Phrase
Author argues that paraphrased idea (PageNumber).

Literary critic Louise Westling argues that Hulga's nihilism is less a genuine philosophical stance than a psychological strategy she adopted to protect herself from vulnerability (48).

Author named in signal phrase, so the page number stands alone in the citation. No quotation marks.

Pattern B · Author in Parenthetical
Paraphrased idea (LastName PageNumber).

One critic has argued that Hulga's nihilism is less a genuine philosophical stance than a psychological strategy she adopted to protect herself from vulnerability (Westling 48).

Author not named in signal phrase, so last name and page number both appear in the citation.

HTML Source Author's Name in Signal Phrase or in Parenthetical
Pattern C · Author in Signal Phrase
Author argues that paraphrased idea.

O'Connor scholar Frederick Asals has observed that the conflict between Hulga and Mrs. Hopewell represents a stalemate between two equally distorted ways of perceiving the world.

HTML source plus author in signal phrase: no parenthetical citation needed at all. Period ends the sentence normally.

Pattern D · Author in Parenthetical
Paraphrased idea (LastName).

One scholar has observed that the conflict between Hulga and Mrs. Hopewell represents a stalemate between two equally distorted ways of perceiving the world (Asals).

HTML source plus author not in signal phrase: last name only in the parenthetical citation.

The Most Common Citation Error

Students sometimes feel that because they rewrote the language, the paraphrase is now “theirs” and no citation is needed. This is incorrect. The scholar's idea belongs to them, not just their words. A paraphrase without a citation is plagiarism. See Section 8 below.

Section Five

Complete Paraphrase Examples: Original, Bad, Good

Each example below shows the original passage from a secondary source used in the sample research essays, followed by a bad paraphrase, then a good paraphrase. Read both. Understanding why the bad paraphrase fails is just as instructive as seeing the good one.

Example 1 · Westling on Hulga's Nihilism

Original Text Westling, p. 48 PDF Article
From the Source

“Hulga's adoption of nihilism functions as a defense mechanism rather than a genuine philosophical position, a way of preempting rejection by rejecting everything first. Her investment in her own intellectual superiority is itself a form of hope, a belief that philosophy can insulate her from human vulnerability.”

✗ Bad Paraphrase

Hulga's embrace of nihilism acts as a defense mechanism rather than a real philosophical stance, a way of avoiding rejection by rejecting everything herself. Her belief in her own intellectual superiority is itself a form of hope, a conviction that philosophy can protect her from human vulnerability.

Why it fails:
  • Patchwriting: “defense mechanism,” “intellectual superiority,” and “human vulnerability” are all borrowed directly from Westling.
  • Word substitution only: “adoption” becomes “embrace” and “functions as” becomes “acts as.” These are synonyms, not a genuine rewrite.
  • No citation: Westling receives no credit at all.
  • Too long: The paraphrase is nearly as long as the original.
✓ Good Paraphrase

Literary critic Louise Westling argues that Hulga's negative worldview is less a genuine belief than a self protective strategy that paradoxically reveals how much she still needs and wants what she claims to have given up (48).

Why it works:
  • Entirely new language: none of Westling's specific phrasing survives, not even “defense mechanism.”
  • Restructured: the sentence enters from a different angle, combining both of Westling's points into a single tighter construction.
  • Shorter: two sentences condensed to one without losing the core argument.
  • Properly cited: author named in signal phrase, page number in parenthetical.

Example 2 · Asals on the Mother Daughter Conflict

Original Text Asals, web article HTML Article
From the Source

“The mother daughter relationship in this story functions as a stalemate between two equally limited ways of seeing, Mrs. Hopewell's optimistic blindness and Hulga's pessimistic one, and the story refuses to endorse either as a reliable window onto reality.”

✗ Bad Paraphrase

According to Asals, the relationship between the mother and daughter works as a stalemate between two equally limited perspectives, Mrs. Hopewell's optimistic blindness and Hulga's pessimistic one, and O'Connor refuses to say either of them sees things correctly.

Why it fails:
  • Patchwriting: “stalemate,” “equally limited,” “optimistic blindness,” and “pessimistic one” are all borrowed directly from Asals.
  • Identical structure: the sentence follows exactly the same shape as the original.
  • The author is correctly named in the signal phrase, so no parenthetical is needed for an HTML source. The real problem is that the language is still too close to the original.
✓ Good Paraphrase

O'Connor scholar Frederick Asals has observed that Hulga and Mrs. Hopewell are caught at an impasse and that O'Connor refuses to provide any convenient answer to the friction between Hulga's negativity and her mother's positivity.

Why it works:
  • New language: “Hulga's negativity and her mother's positivity” replaces “optimistic blindness and pessimistic one” without just swapping synonyms.
  • HTML source plus author in signal phrase: no parenthetical citation needed. Period ends the sentence.

Example 3 · Lev on Luke and Han

Original Text Lev, web article HTML Article
From the Source

“Lucas constructed Luke's alliance with Han as a structural test of the hero's commitment to something beyond personal survival, and the film's deepest dramatic interest lies not in the battle against the Empire but in whether Luke can hold onto his idealism under pressure from the more pragmatic worldview Han represents.”

✗ Bad Paraphrase

Lev argues that Lucas built Luke's relationship with Han as a test of the hero's dedication to something beyond his own survival, and that the movie's real dramatic interest is not the fight against the Empire but whether Luke can keep his idealism under pressure from Han's more pragmatic worldview (Lev).

Why it fails:
  • Patchwriting: “test,” “personal survival,” “dramatic interest,” “idealism,” and “pragmatic worldview” are all lifted from Lev.
  • Identical structure: the two part sentence shape matches the original exactly.
  • Citation error: the author is named in the signal phrase, so the “(Lev)” citation is redundant.
✓ Good Paraphrase

Film scholar Peter Lev has argued that Han's more realistic, and therefore less optimistic, attitude represents another layer of conflict for Luke, whose positivity risks being transformed into pessimism by his alliance with the older smuggler.

Why it works:
  • Scholar identified: “Film scholar Peter Lev” establishes authority and context immediately.
  • HTML plus author in signal phrase: no parenthetical citation needed.
  • Accurate representation: Lev's core argument, that Han functions as a test of Luke's idealism, is preserved and clearly attributed.
Part Two Common Paraphrasing Errors

Common Error · Most Serious

Patchwriting

Patchwriting is the term for a paraphrase that still closely mirrors the original text, using the same sentence structure, the same key phrases, and only substituting individual words for synonyms. It is the most common paraphrasing error, and it is treated as a form of plagiarism even when it is not intentional, because the writing still fundamentally belongs to the original author. The only real fix for patchwriting is to rewrite from scratch using the step by step process in Section 3.

The Test

If you place your paraphrase next to the original and can draw a line connecting most of your words to matching words in the original, it is patchwriting. A genuine paraphrase, placed next to the original, should look structurally and lexically different in every meaningful way, even if the idea is the same.

Original Text Kaminski, p. 172 PDF Article
From the Source

“The refusal of the call was one of the stages Lucas worked hardest to dramatize authentically, understanding it as the moment that establishes what the hero stands to lose by committing, not just comfort, but identity.”

✗ Patchwriting

The refusal of the call was one of the parts that Lucas worked most diligently to portray authentically, seeing it as the moment that shows what the hero has to lose by committing, not just his comfort, but his identity (Kaminski 172).

Every key phrase (“refusal of the call,” “authentically,” “not just comfort, but identity”) is lifted from Kaminski. Only minor substitutions were made.
✓ Genuine Paraphrase

Film historian Michael Kaminski notes that Luke's initial retreat from Obi Wan's call had to communicate what was actually at stake for the hero, namely whom he understood himself to be (172).

The sentence is entirely restructured. No phrases from Kaminski survive. The idea is preserved.

Common Error

Paraphrase Too Long

One of the chief advantages of paraphrasing over quoting is efficiency. A paraphrase lets you incorporate a scholar's multi sentence argument into a single, well placed sentence of your own. If your paraphrase is as long as the original, or nearly so, you have defeated its purpose, and you have likely been working sentence by sentence through the original, which almost always produces patchwriting as a side effect. A well executed paraphrase should typically be shorter than the original passage.

✗ As long as the original

Westling argues that Hulga's philosophy operates as a defense mechanism rather than a genuine philosophical position, meaning she rejects everything as a way of preventing herself from being rejected. On top of this, her sense of intellectual superiority is actually a kind of hope, because she believes her philosophy can shield her from the vulnerabilities of human feeling (48).

This paraphrase is longer than the original. It follows the source sentence by sentence and still borrows key phrases.

✓ Concise: one sentence

Literary critic Louise Westling argues that Hulga's negative worldview is less a genuine belief than a self protective strategy that paradoxically reveals how much she still needs and wants what she claims to have given up (48).

Two original sentences condensed into one. The core insight is preserved; the length is not.

Common Error

Missing Citation on a Paraphrase

The most common paraphrasing error of all: students rewrite the language and then, feeling as though the idea is now “theirs,” leave out the signal phrase or the parenthetical citation. A paraphrase without attribution is plagiarism. The words are yours; the idea belongs to the scholar, and the scholar must be credited. Always include both a signal phrase identifying the source and, for PDF sources, a parenthetical citation with the page number.

✗ No attribution at all

Hulga's negative worldview is less a genuine belief than a self protective strategy that paradoxically reveals how much she still needs and wants what she claims to have given up.

No signal phrase, no citation. This passes Westling's argument off as the student's own idea, which is plagiarism.

✗ Signal phrase but no page number

Literary critic Louise Westling argues that Hulga's negative worldview is less a genuine belief than a self protective strategy that paradoxically reveals how much she still needs and wants what she claims to have given up.

Author named, but this is a PDF source, so the page number must appear in a parenthetical citation.

✓ Fully attributed (PDF source)

Literary critic Louise Westling argues that Hulga's negative worldview is less a genuine belief than a self protective strategy that paradoxically reveals how much she still needs and wants what she claims to have given up (48).

Signal phrase names the scholar; parenthetical gives the page number. Both pieces are required for a PDF source.

✓ Fully attributed (HTML source)

O'Connor scholar Frederick Asals has observed that Hulga and Mrs. Hopewell are caught at an impasse and that O'Connor refuses to provide any convenient answer to the friction between them.

HTML source plus author in signal phrase: no parenthetical citation needed. Attribution still happens through the signal phrase.

Common Error

Inaccurate Paraphrase

A paraphrase that distorts or oversimplifies the scholar's argument is not just a writing error. It misrepresents the source and undermines your essay's credibility. This often happens when a student paraphrases without fully understanding the original, or when the drive to simplify causes nuance to be lost. After writing your paraphrase, re-read the original and ask: Would the scholar recognize this as their argument?

✗ Meaning distorted

Asals argues that Hulga is the smarter of the two women but that O'Connor still does not agree with her worldview (Asals).

This loses Asals's key point entirely. The original argues that both women are equally limited and that neither is endorsed. The paraphrase invents a hierarchy that the source never makes.

✓ Nuance preserved

O'Connor scholar Frederick Asals has observed that Hulga and Mrs. Hopewell are caught at an impasse and that O'Connor refuses to provide any convenient answer to the friction between Hulga's negativity and her mother's positivity.

The essential symmetry of Asals's argument, that both women are equally limited, is preserved in the paraphrase.

Common Error

Paraphrase With No Follow Up Analysis

A paraphrase that is dropped into a paragraph without any follow up analysis is as incomplete as a floating quote. The paraphrase delivers the scholar's point. Your job in the sentence or two that follow is to explain what that point contributes to your argument. Without your analysis, the paraphrase just sits there: evidence without interpretation. The same signal, evidence, analysis structure that applies to direct quotes applies to paraphrases equally.

✗ Paraphrase without analysis

O'Connor scholar Frederick Asals has observed that Hulga and Mrs. Hopewell are caught at an impasse and that O'Connor refuses to provide any convenient answer to the friction between Hulga's negativity and her mother's positivity. This helps explain the conflict in the story.

The final sentence adds nothing. “This helps explain the conflict” tells the reader nothing about what the scholar's point contributes to the student's argument.

✓ Followed by genuine analysis

O'Connor scholar Frederick Asals has observed that Hulga and Mrs. Hopewell are caught at an impasse and that O'Connor refuses to provide any convenient answer to the friction between Hulga's negativity and her mother's positivity. Asals's point reframes the conflict as structural irony rather than a simple clash of intelligence versus stupidity: O'Connor is not presenting Hulga as the clear sighted figure trapped with a foolish woman. She is presenting two forms of willful blindness in unproductive friction.

The analysis explains specifically what Asals's point adds to the essay's argument and connects it to the thesis.

Quick Self Check

Before you finalize a paraphrase, ask three questions: (1) Have I rewritten the language entirely, with no key phrases from the original surviving? (2) Have I cited the source correctly for its format (PDF vs. HTML) and signal phrase choice? (3) Is there a sentence after the paraphrase that explains what the scholar's point contributes to my argument? If you can answer yes to all three, the paraphrase is doing its job.
