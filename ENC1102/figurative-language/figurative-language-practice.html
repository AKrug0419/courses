Poem One

Sonnet 18 — "Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?"

William Shakespeare, 1609

William Shakespeare (1564–1616) was an English playwright, poet, and actor widely regarded as the greatest writer in the English language. His 154 sonnets, published in 1609, are fourteen-line poems written in iambic pentameter exploring themes of love, beauty, time, and immortality. Sonnet 18—"Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?"—is perhaps the most famous poem in the English language. Notice how Shakespeare builds an extended comparison in the opening lines, then systematically dismantles it before arriving at his central claim.

Sonnet 18 — William Shakespeare (1609)
Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?
Thou art more lovely and more temperate.
Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May,
And summer's lease hath all too short a date.

Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines,
And often is his gold complexion dimmed;
And every fair from fair sometime declines,
By chance, or nature's changing course, untrimmed.

But thy eternal summer shall not fade,
Nor lose possession of that fair thou ow'st,
Nor shall death brag thou wand'rest in his shade,
When in eternal lines to Time thou grow'st.

So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see,
So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.

Public domain. First published in Shakespeare's Sonnets, 1609.

Annotated Poem — Figurative Language Identified
Poem Text Figurative Language
Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?
Thou art more lovely and more temperate.
Rough winds do shake the darling buds of May,
And summer's lease hath all too short a date.
Simile (extended)

The entire poem springs from this opening question. "Compare thee to a summer's day" is the explicit simile—you are like a summer's day. Lines 2–12 then test and complicate that comparison.

Personification

"Rough winds" have the human quality of roughness (attitude/mood). The buds are called "darlings"—a term of endearment, as if the winds are bullying beloved children.

Metaphor

"Summer's lease"—summer is a tenant renting time. A lease has an expiration date. Summer doesn't own the season; it only borrows it.

Sometime too hot the eye of heaven shines,
And often is his gold complexion dimmed;
And every fair from fair sometime declines,
By chance, or nature's changing course, untrimmed.
Metaphor

"The eye of heaven" = the sun. This metaphor gives the sky a face. It also implies judgment—an eye watches. "His gold complexion" continues the personification: the sun has a face, a skin tone, a mood.

Personification

"Nature's changing course" is given agency—nature is a force that acts, that trims and untrimmed things. Nature is a gardener or tailor cutting back beauty.

But thy eternal summer shall not fade,
Nor lose possession of that fair thou ow'st,
Nor shall death brag thou wand'rest in his shade,
When in eternal lines to Time thou grow'st.
Metaphor

"Thy eternal summer"—the beloved's beauty is summer, but unlike the real season, theirs will last. The metaphor inverts the opening simile: the beloved isn't just like summer; they contain an immortal version of it.

Personification

"Death brag"—Death is a boastful figure who claims souls as trophies. But the poem's speaker says Death won't get to brag here. Death is given a personality: vain, competitive, gloating.

Metaphor

"Eternal lines to Time" = the poem itself. The sonnet is lines that grow into time—immortalizing the beloved the way a tree grows its rings into history.

So long as men can breathe, or eyes can see,
So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.
Metaphor

"This gives life to thee"—the poem is not just words; it is a living force. The poem gives life. The metaphor completes the argument: writing = immortality.

Step 2 — Analyze the Devices
What is each comparison doing? What are the connotations?
▼
🔒
Your task

For each figurative phrase below, think carefully: what two things are being compared? What qualities does the comparison import? What would be lost if Shakespeare had used plain language instead? Write your thoughts, then reveal each card.

Simile
"Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?"

What qualities does "a summer's day" carry for an Elizabethan reader? Why does Shakespeare immediately admit this comparison is imperfect?

Summer = warmth, beauty, abundance, peak life. But Shakespeare immediately says "thou art more lovely"—the simile is set up only to be rejected. The beloved exceeds summer, which can be too hot, too short, and unreliable. By opening with a comparison the poem then dismantles, Shakespeare argues that conventional comparisons are not good enough for this person.

Metaphor
"summer's lease hath all too short a date"

Summer is a tenant, not an owner. What does calling it a "lease" imply about how nature operates—and what beauty cannot hold onto?

A lease is a temporary agreement. The tenant (summer) must leave when the contract expires. Beauty in the natural world is on borrowed time—it doesn't own itself. The legal vocabulary ("lease," "possession" in line 10) runs throughout the poem, suggesting that ownership of beauty is the central problem Shakespeare is solving through the poem itself.

Metaphor
"the eye of heaven"

The sun is called an "eye." What does this personify about the sky—and what does it imply about the sun's behavior when it gets "too hot" or "dimmed"?

An eye watches, observes, and judges. "Heaven" looking down gives the universe moral attention—the sky sees us. But this eye is also unreliable: it sometimes shines too hot (glaring, oppressive) and sometimes dims (hidden by clouds). The beloved, by contrast, will not be dimmed. The contrast positions the beloved as more constant than even the sun.

Personification
"Nor shall death brag thou wand'rest in his shade"

Death is given a personality here. What kind of person is Death in this image—and why does it matter that Shakespeare imagines Death as specifically boastful?

Death is competitive and vain—a braggart who collects souls as trophies. By giving Death this personality, Shakespeare turns the poem's final argument into a contest that the poem wins. Death won't get to boast about this beloved because the poem preserves them. The personification makes death into a rival the poet can actually defeat—which is the poem's central claim.

Metaphor
"thy eternal summer"

Earlier, being compared to "a summer's day" was inadequate. Now Shakespeare gives the beloved "thy eternal summer." How does returning to the summer metaphor, but modified, change its meaning?

"Eternal" is what natural summer lacks. The regular summer has a short lease; the beloved's version does not. By adding "eternal" to the metaphor, Shakespeare solves the problem raised in the first quatrain. The beloved isn't like an ordinary summer day—they have their own summer that lives forever in the poem. The device completes a circular structure: simile (introduced) → dismantled → rebuilt as metaphor.

Model Answer — Patterns & Connections

Lines 1–8: Comparison → Failure

The opening simile ("compare thee to a summer's day") and its follow-up metaphors ("summer's lease," "eye of heaven") all share one quality: they describe things that are unreliable, temporary, or imperfect. Summer is too short. The sun is too hot or too dim. Nature is "untrimmed." Every figurative device in the first eight lines is introduced to show why it's not good enough. This is Shakespeare's rhetorical setup.

Lines 9–14: Permanence Claimed

"Thy eternal summer," "eternal lines to Time," "this gives life to thee." The second half flips the devices: now the metaphors assert permanence rather than failure. Death is personified but defeated. The poem itself becomes a metaphor for immortality. Each device in the final six lines answers a problem raised in the first eight.

The Central Pattern: Legal Language + Nature

Notice that Shakespeare weaves legal vocabulary throughout the figurative language: "lease," "date," "lose possession," "ow'st." Nature is a tenant who must leave. But the poem argues that art is different—it owns time. The figurative language moves from nature's impermanence to art's permanence, and legal metaphors track that movement. The poem argues, through its own figurative devices, that the act of writing defeats death.

Model Interpretation

One Strong Reading

In Sonnet 18, Shakespeare uses a progression of figurative language—simile, metaphor, and personification—to build and then solve a philosophical problem: how do we preserve what is beautiful when time destroys everything? The poem opens with a simile that it immediately undermines ("Shall I compare thee to a summer's day?"—but summer is too short, too unreliable). Every metaphor in the first eight lines describes something that fails: summer holds its lease briefly; the sun is moody and inconsistent; nature's beauty is "untrimmed" by accident and time. Then the poem pivots. "But thy eternal summer shall not fade" reclaims the metaphor, and "death brag" personifies the poem's greatest enemy—only to defeat it. The argument is not just about a beloved person; it is about what poetry can do. The final couplet makes the claim explicit: "this gives life to thee." Figurative language is not decoration in this sonnet—it is the mechanism of the argument. The poem argues, through its very use of metaphor, that language outlasts everything nature can offer.

A Note on Interpretation

Some readers focus more on the beloved as subject; others on Shakespeare as the poem's real hero (saving his own art from oblivion). Both are defensible readings. What matters is that you ground your interpretation in the specific figurative language—the devices are the evidence.

Poem Two

Hummingbird Abecedarian — Aimee Nezhukumatathil

Originally published in Poem-a-Day, Academy of American Poets, December 17, 2018

Aimee Nezhukumatathil (b. 1974) is a Filipino-South Asian American poet whose work weaves the natural world together with personal history, family, and cultural identity. She is the author of several collections, including Oceanic (Copper Canyon Press, 2018) and the celebrated nature essay collection World of Wonders (2020). "Hummingbird Abecedarian" was originally published in the Academy of American Poets' Poem-a-Day series on December 17, 2018. An abecedarian is a poem whose lines or sections follow the letters of the alphabet in sequence—a form that gives the poem a built-in sense of forward motion, like something arriving letter by letter. Here, the hummingbirds' annual return becomes the occasion for a meditation on what else returns: memory, ancestral connection, and a language the speaker carries silently inside her body without being able to speak it.

Hummingbird Abecedarian — Aimee Nezhukumatathil
Arriving with throats like nipped roses, like a tiny bloom
fastened to each neck, nothing else cuts the air quite
like this thrum to make the small dog at my feet whine and yelp.
So we wait—no excitement pinned to the sky so needled
and our days open full of rain for weeks.
Nothing yet from the ground speaks green except weeds.
But soon you see a familiar shadow hovering
where the glass feeders you brought inside used to hang
because the ice might shatter the pollen junk and leaf bits
collected after this windiest, wildest of winters.
Kin across the ocean surely felt this little jump of blood,
this little heartbeat, perhaps brushed across
my grandmother's mostly grey braid snaked down her brown neck
and back across the Indian and the widest part of the Pacific ocean,
across the Mississippi, and back underneath my patio.
I've lost track of the times I've been silent in my lungs,
quiet as a salamander. Those times I wanted
to decipher the mutter rolled off a stranger's full and beautiful lips.
I only knew they spoke in Malayalam—my father's language—
and how terrific it'd sound if I could make my own slow mouth
ululate like that in utter sorrow or joy.
I'm certain I'd be voracious with each light and peppered syllable
winged back to me in the form of this sort of faith,
a gift like xenia offered to me. And now I must give it back
to this tiny bird, its yield far greener and greater than I could ever repay—
a light like zirconia—hoping for something so simple and sweet to sip.

Copyright © 2018 by Aimee Nezhukumatathil. Originally published in Poem-a-Day on December 17, 2018, by the Academy of American Poets. View at Academy of American Poets.

Annotated Poem — Figurative Language Identified
Poem Text Figurative Language
Arriving with throats like nipped roses, like a tiny bloom
fastened to each neck, nothing else cuts the air quite
like this thrum to make the small dog at my feet whine and yelp.
Simile (doubled)

The ruby-red throat patch of the hummingbird is compared first to "nipped roses" (pinched, delicate, small, blood-red) and then extended: it is "like a tiny bloom fastened to each neck." Two similes in three lines, both reaching for the same small jewel of color. The bird arrives as a living flower pinned to itself.

So we wait—no excitement pinned to the sky so needled
and our days open full of rain for weeks.
Nothing yet from the ground speaks green except weeds.
Metaphor

"Our days open full of rain"—days are containers, like books or boxes, that open to reveal what's inside. These days open and find only rain. The metaphor gives the weeks of waiting a physical heaviness.

Personification

"The ground speaks green"—the earth is given a voice. Green is its language, the vocabulary of spring. The ground is silent until it can finally say something through the color of new growth.

Kin across the ocean surely felt this little jump of blood,
this little heartbeat, perhaps brushed across
my grandmother's mostly grey braid snaked down her brown neck
and back across the Indian and the widest part of the Pacific ocean,
across the Mississippi, and back underneath my patio.
Metaphor (bodily)

"This little jump of blood, this little heartbeat"—the hummingbird sighting is felt as a physical pulse inside the speaker's body. The bird's arrival is not just visual; it is a heartbeat, a jump in the blood. The external event becomes an internal sensation, collapsing the distance between observer and bird.

Metaphor

"Braid snaked down her brown neck"—the grandmother's braid is a snake: sinuous, alive, moving. The image makes the grandmother's body vivid and kinetic across an ocean of distance and time.

I've lost track of the times I've been silent in my lungs,
quiet as a salamander. Those times I wanted
to decipher the mutter rolled off a stranger's full and beautiful lips.
I only knew they spoke in Malayalam—my father's language—
and how terrific it'd sound if I could make my own slow mouth
ululate like that in utter sorrow or joy.
Metaphor

"Silent in my lungs"—silence is located inside the speaker's body, not just in the air around her. This is the silence of a language she knows belongs to her but cannot produce. The lungs—the organ of speech and breath—are the site of this loss.

Simile

"Quiet as a salamander"—salamanders are cold-blooded, still, patient, and often hidden in damp places. To be as quiet as one is to be almost invisible, waiting without urgency. This is a particular kind of silence: not distressed, but watchful.

I'm certain I'd be voracious with each light and peppered syllable
winged back to me in the form of this sort of faith,
a gift like xenia offered to me. And now I must give it back
to this tiny bird, its yield far greener and greater than I could ever repay—
a light like zirconia—hoping for something so simple and sweet to sip.
Metaphor (×2)

"Syllable winged back to me"—language is a bird, returning the way the hummingbird returns. "This sort of faith"—the hummingbird's return is a form of faith, a belief that something will come back even when you've been waiting through rain and cold.

Simile (×2)

"A gift like xenia"—xenia is the ancient Greek concept of hospitality offered to travelers and strangers: a sacred duty of giving. "A light like zirconia"—zirconia is a synthetic crystal that mimics diamond. The hummingbird's light is brilliant, prismatic, almost unreal. Both similes give the bird a cultural weight: ancient, precious, both earned and given.

The figurative language in this poem does more than describe hummingbirds—it does something about memory, distance, and belonging. Try to articulate what, then reveal each card.

Simile
"throats like nipped roses"

A "nipped" rose is pinched, clipped, very small—and red. What does this specific choice of adjective bring to the bird? What does it mean that the bird arrives wearing something like a flower?

"Nipped" means cut back, small, possibly even bitten—it has a quality of precision, even severity. A nipped rose is already a metaphor for brief beauty; the bud hasn't fully opened. By comparing the hummingbird's red throat to this, Nezhukumatathil gives the bird an air of cultivated, almost ornamental beauty—it arrives already adorned. But there's also fragility here: something that can be nipped can be lost. The poem opens with beauty and with the condition of beauty's brevity.

Personification
"Nothing yet from the ground speaks green"

The ground is given a voice, and green is its language. Why might Nezhukumatathil describe the earth's first signs of spring as "speaking" rather than "showing" or "growing"?

Choosing "speaks" rather than "shows" makes green a form of communication—a language. This matters enormously for a poem that is deeply about language: the speaker can't speak her father's language (Malayalam), but she can hear the earth's language of color. Both are forms of speech she is in relationship with. The personification quietly introduces language as the poem's central preoccupation, even before the word "Malayalam" appears.

Metaphor
"this little jump of blood, this little heartbeat"

The hummingbird's arrival is felt as a jump of blood and a heartbeat inside the speaker's body. What does it mean to locate the bird's return as a physical, bodily event—and why does she say her family across the Pacific "surely felt" it too?

By making the hummingbird's return into a jump of blood, Nezhukumatathil argues that some experiences are felt in the body before they are understood in the mind. The metaphor is also ancestral: if kin across the Pacific felt the same jump, then blood here is both literal (the body) and figurative (lineage, family connection). The bird's annual return is a physical reminder of a living thread that runs through oceans and generations. Connection isn't just emotional—it is embodied.

Metaphor
"braid snaked down her brown neck"

The grandmother's braid is a snake. What does comparing a braid to a snake do to the image of this woman? What qualities does a snake bring?

A snake moves, ripples, is alive. Calling the grandmother's braid a snake makes it kinetic—not a static decoration but a living part of her body in motion. The snake also carries ancient associations: wisdom, danger, beauty, earthiness. The metaphor makes the grandmother powerful and vivid across thousands of miles. We feel her body moving. The image also connects to the poem's other images of sinuous, winding paths: the braid "snaked down her brown neck and back" across the Indian Ocean, the Pacific, the Mississippi—as if the braid itself is a river or a migration route.

Simile
"quiet as a salamander"

Of all the quiet animals in the world, why a salamander? What does this specific creature add to the image of the speaker being silent in the presence of Malayalam?

Salamanders live in damp, hidden places—under logs, near water, in the margins of things. They are patient, cold-blooded, almost invisible. To be as quiet as a salamander is to be still in a very particular way: watchful, waiting in the margins, present but unspeaking. The simile captures the speaker's experience of hearing Malayalam without being able to respond to it: she is there, she hears, she understands something is happening, but she has no voice in it. The salamander's cold-bloodedness also suggests the emotional temperature—not hot grief, but a quiet, patient longing.

Simile
"a light like zirconia"

Zirconia is a synthetic crystal—it resembles diamond but is man-made, not dug from the earth. What does it mean to call the hummingbird's light "like zirconia"? Is this a compliment, a complication, or both?

Zirconia is brilliant but artificial—as dazzling as a diamond but made in a lab, not found in nature. Calling the hummingbird's shimmer "like zirconia" is a strange, unexpected choice that keeps the poem from sentimentality. The bird is gorgeous and almost unreal—prismatic, fleeting, something you can barely believe is happening. But zirconia is also accessible, not rare or mined by violence. It is beauty that comes to you, that shows up at your feeder. The simile holds the bird in a double light: natural and almost fabricated, miraculous and ordinary, precious but available to anyone who waits.

Model Answer — Patterns & Connections

Similes: What the Bird Is Like

Every simile in the poem circles the hummingbird and gives it greater richness: it is like nipped roses (delicate, red, brief), a tiny bloom fastened to a neck (ornamental, alive), a gift like xenia (ancient, sacred hospitality), a light like zirconia (brilliant, synthetic, almost unreal). The similes make the bird precious—but also strange, as if it exists slightly outside ordinary nature, more like a gift or a message than an animal.

Metaphors: What Is Felt Inside

"Days open full of rain." "Jump of blood." "Heartbeat." "Braid snaked." "Silent in my lungs." "Syllable winged back." The metaphors all locate experience inside a body—inside lungs, blood, a grandmother's braid. They argue that the things that matter most are felt physically, not just mentally. Connection runs through the body.

The Central Pattern: What Returns

The poem's figurative language is organized around a single question: what comes back? The hummingbirds return every spring. The "jump of blood" is a return of feeling. The braid snaking across oceans is a return of ancestral presence. The syllable "winged back" is a return of language. The personified ground finally "speaks green." Every figurative device describes something that has been absent and then reappears. The poem's subject is not the hummingbird—it is the faith that what matters will keep coming back, even after the longest winter.

Model Interpretation

One Strong Reading

In "Hummingbird Abecedarian," Aimee Nezhukumatathil uses a layered system of figurative language—similes that make the hummingbird precious and strange, metaphors that locate belonging inside the body, personification that gives the natural world a voice—to argue that connection to ancestry is not held in language alone, but in the body's responses to beauty. The poem's structure is a kind of faith: the abecedarian form moves forward letter by letter, the way a season moves forward, the way a hummingbird returns. The similes build the bird into something almost sacred—like a rose, like a bloom, like the ancient gift of xenia, like zirconia—because the speaker needs it to be. The metaphors reveal why: she carries a language she can't speak ("silent in my lungs"), a grandmother she knows across an ocean ("braid snaked"), a family whose blood she shares but whose words she can't produce. What the figurative language collectively argues is that connection doesn't require fluency. The jump of blood when the hummingbird arrives—felt in the body before it can be named—is itself a form of knowing. The bird brings back something the speaker has been waiting for: the proof that what has been separated can still return. That is the poem's "sort of faith."

Connecting the Two Poems

Shakespeare and Nezhukumatathil both use figurative language to argue against loss. Shakespeare defeats death by writing the beloved into a poem that lives forever; Nezhukumatathil accepts that language can be lost while insisting the body still remembers. Both poems ask: what can be preserved? Shakespeare's answer is art. Nezhukumatathil's is something harder to name—the physical faith of a creature that returns each spring, and a body that knows it before the mind can say why.
