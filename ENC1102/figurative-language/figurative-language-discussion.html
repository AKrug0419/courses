Step 2 Analyze the Poem's Figurative Language

Work through the five-step process demonstrated on the previous page. Use the five-step figurative language analysis method from the Defining Figurative Language page. Here are the steps as a quick reference:

Step 1: Locate

Read the poem carefully and identify all key images. Flag any that appear to be figurative language (metaphor, simile, or personification).

Step 2: Analyze Figurative Language

For each metaphor or simile, dissect the comparison: What two things are being compared? What does the comparison reveal or suggest?

Step 3: Engage Regular Images

For any key images that are not figurative language, write down their connotative meanings and associations, as you did in the imagery unit.

Step 4: Connect & Pattern

Look for connections and patterns among the images and figurative devices. Link them to any relevant statement or idea in the poem.

Step 5: Interpret

Formulate a basic interpretation of the poem grounded in your figurative language analysis.

Reminder

Use the sample analyses on the Defining Figurative Language and Figurative Language Sample Analysis pages as your models for how to complete each step.

Step 3 Post Your Analysis

Write up your five-step analysis and submit it to the discussion. Post your analysis to the discussion board. Work through all five steps clearly in your post.

Below is a complete sample response for Charles Simic's “Evening Walk” to use as your model.

Sample Response — Figurative Language Analysis
Charles Simic's “Evening Walk”
Key Metaphors

Trees & humans; trees and stairs; wind & voices/laughter; leaves & mother's lips; birds & lost children.

Figurative Language — Connotative Meanings

The street/surroundings come to life — like his past; some memories are somber; some are happy; poem ends darkly: experience/age makes him realize the loss of innocence; the past is good and bad.

Regular Images — Connotative Meanings

Images suggest a happy memory of a gathering/party; the equally positive happy blue sky at the end contrasts with the more serious final metaphors about lost children.

Connections & Patterns Linked to Ideas

The figurative language brings the setting and the speaker's past to life via personification; both positive and negative associations as he travels mentally through his past.

Basic Interpretation

In “Evening Walk,” Charles Simic uses figurative language to animate his physical surroundings as the speaker moves through the past in his mind. Employing both regular metaphors and similes, the trees, birds, light, and wind all come alive and trigger fleeting memories, both negative and positive. The trees' leaves and the wind evoke memories of his apparently timid mother as well as half-remembered sounds of people gathered somewhere, talking, and then subsequently laughing in hushed tones. Regular images recall a seemingly happy moment of freedom: the bare feet, freed hair, and silk dresses suggest the joviality after or during a party. Despite describing his heart as happy, the speaker still feels a sadness as he recalls these moments “in the shadows” of his memory. Perhaps he misses these former friends and family members? The poem's concluding stanza uses a simile to compare the birds he hears to “lost children” in woods that are growing dark. It's possible the speaker, now that he's older, has learned the sometimes tragic nature of life, a sad detail poisoning his evening stroll. Ultimately, the poem reflects — through revealing figurative language — both how our minds operate as we think upon the past and how our pasts are typically a mixture of both positive and negative associations.
