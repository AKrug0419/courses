What Am I Doing with These Sources?

How primary and secondary sources function as evidence inside your essay

📄 Primary and Secondary Sources: So Now What?

You've probably been asking yourself: "So what am I actually supposed to do with these sources?"

Legit question.

Both kinds of sources — your primary source (the short story) and your secondary sources (the scholarly articles) — function as evidence in your essay to help prove your thesis. Just like the quotes you used in your first essay helped support your argument, you'll now also be quoting from the articles you found in the databases. That's the only real difference between your first essay and this one.

⚖️ Think of It Like a Trial

Because your secondary sources are written by literature experts, they function like expert witnesses in a courtroom. A prosecutor calls expert witnesses whose expertise helps prove the defendant is guilty; a defense attorney calls experts who support the argument of innocence. In your essay, your secondary sources are your expert witnesses: their scholarly authority lends additional weight to the argument you are making about the story.

🔬 How Sources Work Inside a Body Paragraph

Before you start selecting quotes, understand where sources fit inside a body paragraph and what function each piece of evidence is performing. Every well-constructed body paragraph in a research essay follows a predictable pattern — even when the specific content varies.

Here is the anatomy of a typical research essay body paragraph. Notice that the student's own analysis appears after every piece of evidence — sources never speak for themselves:

① Topic Sentence

States the single focused argument of this paragraph — what specific aspect of the story you're analyzing, and how it connects to your thesis. No sources yet. This sentence is entirely your own claim.

② Primary Quote

A brief, specific quote from the story that directly relates to this paragraph's topic. Introduce it with a signal phrase and follow it with an MLA in-text citation.

③ Your Analysis

Your words, your thinking. Explain what the quote means, why it matters, and how it supports your paragraph's argument. Sources never explain themselves.

④ Primary Quote

A second brief quote from the story, supporting the same paragraph argument from a different angle. Use a different signal phrase than your first.

⑤ Your Analysis

Your explanation of the second primary source quote. Connect it to the first and show how it develops your paragraph's argument.

⑥ Secondary Source

A quote or paraphrase from one of your scholarly articles that supports your paragraph's argument. Introduce with a strong signal phrase that names the scholar and follow with a parenthetical citation.

⑦ Your Analysis

Your explanation of what the scholar is saying and why it supports your specific argument. Don't just summarize the quote — analyze how it functions as evidence.

⑧ Secondary Source

A second quote or paraphrase from another scholarly article. Vary your signal verbs and phrasing to avoid repetition across the essay.

⑨ Your Analysis

Your explanation of the second scholar's contribution to the paragraph argument.

⑩ Closing Sentence

A sentence that wraps up the paragraph's argument and pivots toward the next paragraph or gestures back to the thesis. No new evidence here.

📍 Where Do the Secondary Source Quotes Go?

Most of your secondary source quotes belong inside your body paragraphs, placed after your primary source evidence and your own analysis. While you're allowed to include a secondary source quote in your introduction or conclusion, this is optional. Focus on placing them where they can do the most argumentative work.

📌 How Many Secondary Source Quotes Do You Need?
📌 Six Secondary Source Quotes — Total, Across All Three Articles

Over the length of your entire essay, you will quote six times total from your three secondary sources combined — not six from each. Of those six, four should be direct quotes and two should be paraphrases.

🔎 How to Select Your Secondary Source Quotes

Not every passage in a scholarly article is worth quoting. Before you can decide what to use, you need to know what you're looking for. The steps below walk you through the most efficient approach to reading your secondary sources.

Step 1

Read your outline first — know your paragraph arguments before you open the article

Academic database searches often return articles broadly about your author or story but that may not directly serve your specific argument. Before you open an article, remind yourself of your three body paragraph topics.

Hurston — "Sweat"Power and control → Delia's labor as autonomy → Snake symbolism and liberation
Carter — "Company of Wolves"Gender stereotypes → Female intelligence and sexuality → Self-determined transformation
Roth — "Conversion of the Jews"Ozzie's internal conflict → External conflicts → Rooftop ending and moral courage

💡 A passage about your story is only useful if it directly supports one of your specific paragraph arguments. Reading with your outline open prevents you from flagging passages that have nowhere to go.

Step 2

Read the article in two passes — skim first, then read closely

Scholarly articles are written by academics for other academics. Reading one cold is exhausting and often discouraging. A two-pass approach makes this much more manageable.

🔎 Pass One: Skim for Relevance (5–10 min)
  • Abstract or opening paragraph — what is the article's main argument?
  • Section headings — which sections look relevant?
  • First and last sentences of each paragraph
  • Conclusion — what does the scholar ultimately claim?
📄 Pass Two: Targeted Close Reading

Go back and read only the sections your skim flagged as relevant. Read slowly — you're looking for specific sentences where the scholar makes a clear, focused claim about the story.

You don't need to understand the entire article. You need to understand the specific passage you plan to use well enough to explain it in your own words afterward.
Step 3

Annotate as you read — flag, connect, and note

Don't read and hope you'll remember what was useful. Highlight or underline directly in the PDF, or keep a notes document open alongside the article. For each passage you flag, note which body paragraph it could support — this will save significant time when you start drafting.

Use It or Skip It?

✓ Use it if…
  • It makes a specific interpretive claim about your story
  • It connects directly to one of your body paragraph topics
  • You can explain in your own words what the scholar is saying
  • You can articulate how it helps prove your argument
  • It's short enough to quote cleanly, or condensable into a paraphrase
✗ Skip it if…
  • It's biographical context, plot summary, or publication history
  • It's about a different work by the same author
  • You can't explain what the scholar is actually arguing
  • You can't connect it to any of your specific paragraph claims
  • Quoting it would require so much setup that it disrupts the paragraph
