Sample Research Essay Outline

The Supernatural and the Limits of Knowledge in Vladimir Nabokov's "The Vane Sisters" — Three Body Paragraphs

💡 How to Use This Sample Outline

This sample outline models the structure your own outline should follow. Each body paragraph entry identifies a distinct aspect of the essay's central theme, outlines the evidence that will be used, and maps the analytical work that needs to happen around that evidence.

This is a research essay outline: every body paragraph must incorporate a quote from the short story (primary source), at least one quote or paraphrase from a scholarly article (secondary source), and your own analysis explaining how the evidence supports your argument. This differs from your first essay this semester, which required only primary source quotes.

🔖 Connecting to Prewriting & Planning

Your outline is the bridge between prewriting and drafting. The three body paragraph topics in this sample grew out of close reading of "The Vane Sisters" — the same kind of analytical attention you brought to your study question responses. Use your own notes and responses as the raw material for your body paragraph topics.

🔬 Body Paragraph Structure (Research Essay)

Every body paragraph in your research essay should follow this six-part structure. The sample outline below is organized around this anatomy. As you plan each of your own paragraphs, use these six steps as your checklist.

① Topic Sentence

Your own claim about what aspect of the theme you're analyzing and how it connects to the essay's argument. No sources here.

② Primary Quote

A brief, specific quote from the short story that directly supports this paragraph's topic. Introduce with a signal phrase; include MLA citation.

③ Your Analysis

Explain what the evidence means, why it matters, and how it supports your topic sentence. Sources never explain themselves.

④ Secondary Quote or Paraphrase

A brief direct quote or paraphrase from a scholarly article that supports your point. Introduce the scholar by name; include MLA citation.

⑤ Your Analysis

Explain how the scholar's point deepens your argument — not just that they agree, but how and why their insight strengthens this paragraph's specific claim.

⑥ Closing Sentence

Wrap up the paragraph's argument and gesture toward the next paragraph's topic. Your own voice only — no new quotes.

🔑 Your Voice Leads

Your analysis should take up more space than your quotes. If quotes dominate a paragraph, add more of your own thinking.

🔑 Introduce Before You Quote

Never drop a quote into a paragraph without a signal phrase that tells the reader who is speaking and why it matters.

🔑 Analyze After You Quote

Every quote needs your explanation immediately after it. Tell the reader what it means and how it proves your point.

📄 The Sample Outline
I. Introduction Paragraph

Topic Selected: The Supernatural and the Limits of Knowledge in Vladimir Nabokov's "The Vane Sisters."

Note: You do not need to write the full introduction in your outline. Simply identify your topic, the author, and the title as shown here.

II. Body Paragraph One: The Narrator's Rational Skepticism and Its Limits
① Topic Sentence Direction

The unnamed narrator of "The Vane Sisters" is a self-described rationalist who dismisses the possibility of any supernatural reality — but his confident skepticism is quietly and systematically undermined by the events of the story, including details he fails to register consciously.

② Primary Source Evidence
A. Evidence of the narrator's dismissiveness toward the supernatural
His condescending attitude toward Sybil's belief in spirit communication — select a brief quote showing his tone when he describes her faith in occult signs and messages
His reaction to D.'s séance activities — evidence that he regards the whole enterprise as embarrassing self-delusion rather than legitimate experience
B. A moment where the narrative itself contradicts his certainty
The icicle passage at the opening — select a brief quote from the description of the shadow on the sidewalk, which Nabokov has noted encodes hidden meaning the narrator does not perceive
What his failure to "read" this image demonstrates about the gap between what he observes and what he understands
③ Your Analysis

Explain how Nabokov establishes the narrator's rationalism as the story's central dramatic irony: the reader is positioned to perceive what the narrator cannot. What does this structural choice suggest about the relationship between intelligence, certainty, and genuine knowledge?

④ Secondary Source Evidence

Plan to include here: a brief quote or paraphrase from one of your scholarly articles that addresses Nabokov's use of unreliable narration, his interest in hidden patterning, or the story's treatment of perception and knowledge. Introduce the scholar by name before the quote.

Placeholder: According to [Scholar's Last Name], "[relevant quote]" ([page number]).

⑤ Your Analysis of Secondary Source

Connect the scholar's reading of Nabokov's narrative technique to your argument about the narrator's limited perception — how does their insight about the story's structural design or Nabokov's style strengthen your claim that rationalism is being exposed rather than endorsed?

⑥ Closing Sentence Direction

Summarize the argument about the narrator's compromised perspective and transition toward the sisters themselves — whose experiences with the supernatural the narrator has dismissed but whose influence on the story's structure cannot be explained away.

III. Body Paragraph Two: The Sisters as Agents of Hidden Meaning
① Topic Sentence Direction

Cynthia and Sybil Vane are not passive victims in the narrator's account but active presences — the story suggests that the dead sisters continue to shape and encode the narrative itself, embedding meaning beneath its surface that the narrator cannot consciously access.

② Primary Source Evidence
A. Cynthia's belief in "intervenient" supernatural signs during her life
Select a brief quote from the narrator's description of Cynthia's system of reading omens and coincidences — her conviction that the dead communicate through small disruptions in daily life
The specific "laws" of her supernatural worldview, as the narrator summarizes them — what she believed the dead could and could not do
B. Evidence that the sisters' influence is encoded in the narrative structure
The famous acrostic in the story's final paragraph: the first letters of each word spell out a message from Cynthia and Sybil — reference this without quoting the full passage; note that Nabokov confirmed this hidden message in correspondence
What it means that the narrator's own words carry a meaning he does not intend or perceive — how this literalizes the story's supernatural theme at the level of the text itself
③ Your Analysis

Explain how Nabokov transforms the sisters from subjects of the narrator's story into co-authors of it. The acrostic is not merely a puzzle — it is a formal argument: if meaning can be hidden in plain sight within language itself, what does the story suggest about the limits of any single observer's ability to grasp the full reality of what they're reading or experiencing?

④ Secondary Source Evidence

Plan to include here: a brief quote or paraphrase from one of your scholarly articles that addresses the story's hidden acrostic, Nabokov's use of concealment and layers of meaning, or the sisters as active narrative presences. Introduce the scholar by name before the quote.

Placeholder: According to [Scholar's Last Name], "[relevant quote]" ([page number]).

⑤ Your Analysis of Secondary Source

Connect the scholar's analysis of the story's hidden architecture to your argument about the sisters as agents — how does their reading of the acrostic, or of Nabokov's layered technique, deepen the claim that knowledge in this story is always partial and that the supernatural operates through the very medium of language?

⑥ Closing Sentence Direction

Wrap up the sisters-as-agents argument and transition toward the story's broader implications: if the dead can encode messages in the narrator's own words without his awareness, what does the story ultimately argue about the relationship between knowledge, reality, and what lies beyond rational perception?

IV. Body Paragraph Three: What the Story Argues About Knowledge and Its Limits
① Topic Sentence Direction

Taken as a whole, "The Vane Sisters" does not simply present the supernatural as a possibility — it uses the narrator's blind spots and the story's hidden architecture to argue that knowledge is always incomplete, that the most important meanings may be invisible to those who are most certain they see clearly.

② Primary Source Evidence
A. The narrator's final state of mind
Select a brief quote from the story's closing paragraphs — the narrator's dream, or his waking reflection, that captures his continued inability to perceive what the reader now knows is encoded in his own language
His tone as the story ends: does he feel satisfied, unsettled, or still confidently detached? What does his emotional state reveal about his relationship with the unknown?
B. The story's final irony
The contrast between what the narrator believes he has told — a story about grief, a strange friendship, and a minor errand — and what the reader understands he has actually delivered: a posthumous message from the dead
What this gap between the narrator's intention and the story's actual meaning implies about consciousness, agency, and what we cannot know about our own experience
③ Your Analysis

Explain why Nabokov's choice to leave the narrator completely unaware of what he has transmitted is the story's most important thematic statement. The rational mind, the story suggests, is not a reliable instrument for measuring the full range of what exists — and the most devastating thing about this limitation is that it cannot be perceived from the inside. What does this argue about the epistemological overconfidence that rationalism can produce?

④ Secondary Source Evidence

Plan to include here: a brief quote or paraphrase from one of your scholarly articles that addresses the story's thematic conclusion, Nabokov's skepticism about rational certainty, or the story's treatment of death, consciousness, and what lies beyond. Introduce the scholar by name before the quote.

Placeholder: According to [Scholar's Last Name], "[relevant quote]" ([page number]).

⑤ Your Analysis of Secondary Source

Connect the scholar's reading of the story's philosophical or thematic stakes to your argument about the limits of knowledge — how does their analysis of Nabokov's relationship to the supernatural, or to the problem of consciousness, reinforce your claim about what the story ultimately argues?

⑥ Closing Sentence Direction

Synthesize the argument about knowledge and its limits and gesture toward the conclusion's broader claim: that "The Vane Sisters" uses the supernatural not as a cheap trick but as a rigorous formal and philosophical argument about the incompleteness of any observer's perspective.

LAST. Conclusion Paragraph

You can leave this blank in your outline. The conclusion is typically easier to write after the body paragraphs are fully drafted, since it synthesizes arguments you've already made.

📌
Secondary Source Distribution Reminder

Over the full essay, you will quote six times total from your three secondary sources combined. In a three-body-paragraph essay, a simple distribution is two secondary source quotes per body paragraph. In a four-body-paragraph essay, you might distribute as 2 + 2 + 1 + 1 — six total either way.

As you build your outline, note next to each secondary source slot which of your three articles you plan to draw from. Planning this during the outline stage will save significant time during drafting.
