Select a Film

The college’s Film Streaming Library includes three films that work well for a dystopian research essay. Review the descriptions and dystopian connections below, then choose the one you want to analyze.

Content Advisory

All three films are Rated R and contain adult language, violence, and/or mature themes. If you are unfamiliar with a title, please research it before watching to ensure the content aligns with your personal preferences.

Children of Men

Sci-Fi / Thriller • 2006 • Alfonso Cuarón   Rated R

In 2027, humanity faces extinction: no child has been born in eighteen years. Governments have collapsed, and Britain — the last functioning nation-state — maintains order through militarized borders, mass deportation of refugees, and a pervasive atmosphere of despair. When a disillusioned bureaucrat named Theo is recruited to escort a miraculously pregnant woman to safety, his journey forces him to confront the cost of a society that has traded compassion for control.

Dystopian Connections

The film is one of the most analytically rich dystopian texts in modern cinema. Britain’s government maintains power through bureaucratic and technological control: refugee cages, propaganda broadcasts, and checkpoints are woven into everyday life. Dehumanization operates on a mass scale as immigrants are stripped of identity and confined to camps. The natural world feels depleted — grey, polluted, and indifferent — reinforcing the narrative’s sense that humanity has exhausted itself. Theo functions as a classic dystopian protagonist: he begins the film numb and disengaged, but is gradually forced to question a system he had passively accepted and to act on a moral conviction he thought he had lost.

Pan’s Labyrinth

Fantasy / Drama • 2006 • Guillermo del Toro   Rated R

Spain, 1944. The Civil War is over, but Captain Vidal — a fascist officer stationed in a rural mill — wages a brutal campaign to crush the last of the Republican resistance. His new stepdaughter, Ofelia, a young girl who finds solace in fairy tales, discovers a crumbling labyrinth on the estate and is told by a faun that she is the reincarnation of an underworld princess. To reclaim her throne, she must complete three dangerous tasks — but the real monsters in Ofelia’s world wear military uniforms.

Dystopian Connections

Del Toro constructs a society governed by philosophical and military control: Captain Vidal enforces Franco’s ideology through terror, torture, and absolute obedience to hierarchy. Propaganda and nationalist rhetoric justify violence as patriotic duty. Freedom is restricted at every level — the rebels are hunted, the servants are surveilled, and Ofelia is told where to go, what to wear, and how to behave. Forced conformity is the regime’s organizing principle: individuality and resistance are treated as diseases to be eradicated. Ofelia functions as a dystopian protagonist who, despite her youth, believes something is terribly wrong with the world around her and whose acts of imaginative disobedience expose the system’s cruelty to the audience.

Freedom Restricted Forced Conformity Propaganda Philosophical / Military Control Dystopian Protagonist

The Matrix

Sci-Fi / Action • 1999 • The Wachowskis   Rated R

Thomas Anderson is a programmer by day and a hacker by night who has always sensed that something is wrong with the world. When a mysterious figure named Morpheus offers him the truth, Anderson — now Neo — discovers that the reality he knows is a simulated prison: a digital dreamworld called the Matrix, built by machines to keep humanity sedated while their bodies are harvested for energy. Freed from the simulation, Neo must decide whether he can become the person the resistance believes he is.

Dystopian Connections

The Matrix is one of the most complete dystopian thought experiments in cinema. Technological control is total: machines have replaced human civilization and built a simulated reality so convincing that billions of people live and die inside it without suspecting the truth. The illusion of utopia is the simulation itself — a late‑twentieth‑century world that feels ordinary precisely because it was designed to suppress resistance. Dehumanization is literal: human beings are reduced to biological batteries, grown in pods, and discarded when depleted. Surveillance saturates the Matrix in the form of Agents, programs that can inhabit any citizen at any moment. Neo is a textbook dystopian protagonist: he feels trapped, questions the system before anyone tells him to, develops moral conviction, and — critically — helps the audience see the architecture of control they might otherwise accept as normal.

Illusion of Utopia Dehumanization Surveillance Technological Control Dystopian Protagonist
Tips for Choosing

All three films are equally viable for this assignment — none is significantly harder or easier than the others. If you have already seen one of these films, that can be an advantage: familiarity with the plot lets you focus on analysis rather than comprehension during your viewing. If you haven’t seen any of them, choose the one whose premise interests you most. Interest is the best predictor of a strong essay.

SEC·04

Watching with an Analytical Eye

Screening a film for a research essay is more involved than watching for entertainment. You’ll want to watch actively, with the analytical vocabulary from the Dystopian Literature & Film module in mind. Plan for this to take longer than the film’s runtime.

Recommended Strategy
  • First viewing: Watch the film all the way through and let yourself experience it as a story
  • Second pass: Return to key scenes and take notes — pause, rewind, and identify specific dystopian characteristics, control types, and protagonist traits
  • Take breaks between sessions so your observations stay sharp
What to Look For
  • Which dystopian characteristics are most visible in the film? (surveillance, propaganda, dehumanization, etc.)
  • What type of control does the society use, and how is it maintained?
  • How does the protagonist respond to the system — and which of the four traits do they exhibit?
  • What is the film arguing about the world it came from?
Key Concept

The analytical lenses you practiced during the Minority Report screening are the same ones you’ll use here: characteristics of dystopian societies, types of dystopian control, and traits of the dystopian protagonist. The lenses are the same — the film is yours to choose.
