Sample Research Essay Outline

The Male Gaze and Female Self-Transformation in Angela Carter's "The Tiger's Bride" — Three Body Paragraphs

💡 How to Use This Sample Outline

This sample outline models the structure your own outline should follow. It is based on Angela Carter's "The Tiger's Bride" from The Bloody Chamber — a story you are not writing about. Use it only to understand the structure and level of detail expected in your own outline.

Notice how each body paragraph is organized: it begins with a brief phrase identifying the paragraph's focus, moves through two rounds of primary evidence and analysis, and includes marked spaces for secondary sources that you will fill in later during the research stage.

📌 Secondary Sources: Leave Space Now, Fill In Later

You have not yet learned to do literary research. Do not worry about finding secondary sources for your outline. Simply leave a clearly marked placeholder — as shown in this sample — where each secondary source quote will eventually go. You will locate and add those sources in the next stage of the writing process.

🔬 Body Paragraph Structure (Research Essay)

Every body paragraph in your research essay follows this ten-step structure. You will quote directly from the story two to three times per paragraph and leave space for secondary sources throughout. The sample outline below is organized around this structure.

① Topic Sentence

Your own claim about what aspect of the theme you're analyzing. No sources here.

② Primary Evidence

A specific quote from the short story that directly supports this paragraph's topic. Introduce with a signal phrase; include MLA citation.

③ Your Analysis

Explain what the evidence means and how it supports your topic sentence. Sources never explain themselves.

④ Possible Secondary Source

Leave a placeholder here. You will add a quote or paraphrase from a scholarly article during the research stage.

⑤ Your Analysis

When you fill in the secondary source, you will explain how the scholar's point deepens your argument.

⑥ Primary Evidence

A second (or third) quote from the story, developing the paragraph's argument further.

⑦ Your Analysis

Explain what this second piece of evidence adds to the paragraph's claim.

⑧ Possible Secondary Source

Leave a second placeholder here if needed. Not every paragraph requires two secondary source slots.

⑨ Your Analysis

When you fill in the second secondary source, you will explain how it strengthens this paragraph's specific claim.

⑩ Closing Sentence

Wrap up the paragraph's argument and gesture toward the next paragraph. Your own voice only — no new quotes.

🔑 Your Voice Leads

Your analysis should take up more space than your quotes. If quotes dominate, add more of your own thinking.

🔑 Introduce Before You Quote

Never drop a quote in without a signal phrase. Tell the reader who is speaking and why it matters.

🔑 Analyze After You Quote

Every quote needs your explanation immediately after it — tell the reader what it means and how it proves your point.

📄 The Sample Outline
I. Introduction Paragraph

Topic Selected: The Male Gaze and Female Self-Transformation in Angela Carter's "The Tiger's Bride."

Note: You do not need to write the full introduction in your outline. Simply identify your topic, the author, and the title as shown above.

II. Body Paragraph One: The Beast's Gaze as an Instrument of Objectification
① Topic Sentence
The Beast's demand to see the narrator unclothed — and her refusal — establishes the male gaze as an instrument of objectification that reduces women to passive objects for male viewing pleasure
② Primary Evidence
A. The Beast's demand — her father has lost her in a card game
"My father lost me to The Beast at cards."(Carter 51)
The transaction reduces her to property — she is staked and lost like an object, not a person
B. The Beast's specific request: to see her naked
"The Beast wants nothing but the sight of me."(Carter 55)
The demand is visual, not physical — the male gaze itself is the violation Carter is interrogating
③ Your Analysis
Explain how Carter establishes the narrator as a commodity passed between men — first by her father, then by the Beast — and how the Beast's request literalizes the logic of objectification: to see a woman is to possess her
Connect this to the story's broader feminist argument: in a world governed by male exchange, a woman's body belongs to whoever is looking at it

III. Body Paragraph Two: The Narrator Looks Back — Reversing the Gaze
① Topic Sentence
When the narrator begins to look at the Beast with genuine curiosity — rather than fear or disgust — Carter stages a reversal of the power dynamic: the woman becomes an active observer, and the objectifying gaze begins to dissolve
② Primary Evidence
A. The narrator's description of the Beast — she studies him closely
"He was a carnival figure made of nothing so much as a wish to be seen."(Carter 60)
The Beast, who positioned himself as the powerful viewer, is now revealed as himself wanting to be seen — the desire to be perceived is mutual
Her act of looking carefully at him is an act of recognition, not possession — she sees him as a subject, not an object
③ Your Analysis
Explain how Carter signals the shift in power through the narrator's gaze: when she studies the Beast rather than shrinking from him, she claims the authority of the observer — the same authority he held over her earlier in the story
What does Carter suggest by making the Beast himself vulnerable to being seen? How does this complicate the story's initial power dynamic?

⑥ Primary Evidence
B. The narrator undresses herself — on her own terms
"I will dress myself in the garment of my own free will."(Carter 63)
Her undressing is not submission to the Beast's demand but an act of autonomous self-disclosure — she controls the conditions under which she is seen
The language of will and choice is central — Carter emphasizes that self-exposure can be an act of power, not vulnerability
⑦ Your Analysis

IV. Body Paragraph Three: Transformation as Liberation — Becoming Animal, Becoming Self
① Topic Sentence
The narrator's transformation into a tiger at the story's climax is not a loss of humanity but an act of radical self-reclamation — Carter argues that the authentic self exists beneath the social roles and performances that patriarchal culture demands of women
② Primary Evidence
A. The Beast licks away her human skin — layer by layer
"Each stroke of his tongue ripped off skin after successive skin, all the skins of a life in the world."(Carter 64)
"Skins of a life in the world" — the human skin is not her true self but a series of socially imposed identities
The stripping away is described as relief rather than violation — the accumulated roles of daughter, commodity, and female object fall away
③ Your Analysis
Explain how Carter frames the removal of human skin as a feminist argument: femininity, in this story, is not natural but constructed — a series of performances layered over a more authentic self
What does the animal beneath those layers represent? Why does Carter choose the tiger — a predator, not prey — as the narrator's true form?

⑥ Primary Evidence
B. The final image — she is tiger, not woman
"And I was nobody's meat anymore."(Carter 65)
The phrase "nobody's meat" echoes the story's opening — her father lost her at cards as if she were property
As a tiger, she is predator, not prey — the terms of the original power dynamic are entirely reversed
⑦ Your Analysis
Explain the significance of the final line in relation to the story's opening: the narrator began as "meat" — a commodity gambled by her father — and ends as a tiger, beyond the reach of ownership or the male gaze
What does Carter argue about the cost of liberation? Does the narrator gain freedom by leaving the human world entirely — and what does that suggest about the possibilities for women within patriarchal society?

LAST. Conclusion Paragraph

You can leave this blank in your outline. The conclusion is typically easier to write after the body paragraphs are fully drafted, since it synthesizes arguments you've already made.

📌
Secondary Source Placeholders — A Reminder

You do not need to find secondary sources for your outline. Simply leave a clearly marked placeholder in each spot where a secondary source quote will eventually go — just as the sample above does. Over the full essay, you will quote six times total from your three secondary sources combined. In a three-body-paragraph essay, plan for two secondary source placeholders per body paragraph. In a four-body-paragraph essay, you might distribute as 2 + 2 + 1 + 1.
