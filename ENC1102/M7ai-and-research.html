AI and Academic Research

What AI Can and Can't Do for You — and Why the Difference Matters

Orientation

AI Tools Are Everywhere. You Need to Understand What They Actually Are.

You've almost certainly used AI writing tools — ChatGPT, Google Gemini, Perplexity, Microsoft Copilot, and others. These tools have become genuinely useful for a wide range of tasks, and they're only going to become more present in academic and professional life. Refusing to engage with them isn't a realistic or useful approach.

But using AI tools well — especially for academic research — requires understanding what they actually do. And what they actually do is quite different from what they seem to do.

What AI Language Models Actually Are

Tools like ChatGPT are called large language models (LLMs). They work by predicting, based on enormous amounts of training data, what words are most likely to follow a given sequence of words. This makes them extraordinarily good at generating fluent, confident, authoritative-sounding prose.

What they are not is a search engine, a database, or a fact-checker. They don't look things up. They generate plausible-sounding text based on patterns in their training data. This means they can produce text that sounds correct but is factually wrong — and because the text sounds fluent and confident, those errors are easy to miss.

✦
The Most Important Problem

AI Hallucination — and Why It Matters for Your Essay

The most serious problem with using AI for academic research is called hallucination: AI tools confidently generate information that is simply false, including citations for sources that do not exist. This isn't a bug that will eventually be fixed — it's a fundamental feature of how large language models work. Because the model is predicting plausible text rather than retrieving verified facts, it fills in gaps with confident fabrications.

Studies of AI-generated citations have found that approximately one in five citations produced by AI tools are completely fabricated — not approximations of real sources, but entirely invented author names, journal titles, volume numbers, and page ranges. Another significant percentage contain errors that make them uncitable. The fabrications are not flagged or disclaimed. The AI presents them in exactly the same confident tone as accurate information.

Why This Is a Serious Academic Integrity Issue

If you submit a paper with AI-generated citations that you haven't verified, you may be citing sources that don't exist — which constitutes academic fraud whether you intended it or not. Every semester, students at colleges across the country receive failing grades or academic misconduct charges for submitting papers with fabricated sources they got from AI tools and never checked. Ignorance of the hallucination problem is not a defense.

What AI Hallucination Looks Like: A Demonstration

Below is a realistic example of the kind of response you might get if you asked an AI tool to help you find literary criticism of Gwendolyn Brooks's "the mother." Read it carefully — then look at the annotations.

Demonstration
A Realistic AI Response — and Its Hidden Problems
You
Can you suggest some scholarly articles about Gwendolyn Brooks's poem "the mother" that I could use for a research essay?
AI Tool
Sure! Here are several scholarly articles that analyze Gwendolyn Brooks's "the mother" and would be appropriate for a literary research essay:
Johnson, Patricia L. "Apostrophe and Elegy: Gwendolyn Brooks's 'the mother' and the Politics of Grief." African American Review, vol. 44, no. 2, 2011, pp. 215–229.
⚠
This article does not exist. "Patricia L. Johnson" returns no results as a scholar in MLA International Bibliography or any library database. The journal and volume number are real, but this article is not in that volume.
Williams, Marcus T. "Maternal Elegy in the Early Work of Gwendolyn Brooks." PMLA, vol. 118, no. 3, 2003, pp. 601–615.
⚠
Also fabricated. No article by this title or author appears in PMLA vol. 118. The page range is plausible; the source is not real.
You might also find relevant discussion in Callaloo and Ploughshares, both of which have published criticism of Brooks's work...
⚠
Both journals are real. This observation is accurate. But notice: the AI's confident, accurate mention of real journals gives the whole response an air of reliability — even though the specific citations it provided are fabricated.
What Makes This Dangerous

The citations in the demonstration above look exactly like real scholarly citations. They have author names, article titles, journal names, volume numbers, issue numbers, publication years, and page ranges — all formatted correctly in MLA style. A student who didn't know to check would copy them directly into a Works Cited and submit a paper citing sources that don't exist.

This is why the verification principle — discussed below — is non-negotiable. Every citation from an AI tool must be verified in a library database before it appears in your work.

Formative Check — Think First, Then Reveal
1What is AI hallucination, and why does it happen?
Answer

AI hallucination is when an AI language model generates information that is false — including fabricated citations, invented quotes, or incorrect facts — and presents it with the same confident tone as accurate information. It happens because AI models don't retrieve verified information; they predict plausible text based on patterns in their training data. When they encounter gaps in their training data, they fill those gaps with what looks like a plausible answer — which may bear no relationship to reality.

2A classmate says: "I asked ChatGPT for sources, and the journal names it gave me look legit — I can tell it's not making them up." What's wrong with this reasoning?
Answer

The journal names may well be real — AI tools have learned the names of legitimate academic journals. But a real journal name does not mean the specific article, author, volume, or page range is real. As the demonstration on this page shows, AI can pair a completely fabricated article with an accurate journal name. Your classmate is making the same error as reading a suspicious website carefully and concluding it must be credible because the writing sounds professional: the surface features of credibility are not evidence of actual credibility.

3Why is submitting an AI-generated citation without verifying it an academic integrity issue, even if you didn't intend to fabricate anything?
Answer

Academic integrity requires that the work you submit — including your citations — accurately represents actual scholarship. A citation to a non-existent source is a false claim regardless of how it got into your paper. The standard the college applies is whether the work is accurate, not whether you meant to submit inaccurate work. This is why verification is non-negotiable: submitting AI content you haven't checked transfers the responsibility for the AI's errors to you.

What AI Is Actually Good For

Legitimate — and Illegitimate — Uses of AI in Your Research

The hallucination problem doesn't mean AI tools have no role in academic work. It means their role is specific and limited — and that understanding where AI is genuinely useful will actually make you a better researcher, not a dependent one.

Think of AI as a research companion rather than a research source. It's excellent at helping you prepare to do research and reflect on what you've found — but it's not a substitute for the actual research itself.

✓
Appropriate Uses
  • Brainstorming research angles. Ask AI to suggest interpretive angles or thematic lenses for a poem you're writing about — then pursue those angles in library databases with actual scholarship.
  • Generating search keywords. Ask AI: "What keywords and phrases would be useful for finding literary criticism of Emily Dickinson's 'Because I could not stop for Death'?" The suggestions can jump-start your database search.
  • Understanding unfamiliar terminology. If a scholarly article uses a critical theory term you don't recognize, ask AI to explain it in plain language.
  • Background orientation. Get a quick general overview of a poet's life and historical moment to give your reading context — then verify key facts through reliable sources.
  • Thinking through your argument. Describe your essay's thesis to an AI tool and ask it to identify potential counterarguments or weaknesses you should address.
✕
Inappropriate Uses
  • Finding research sources. Do not ask AI to provide citations. As this page demonstrates, AI citations are frequently fabricated. Use library databases and Google Scholar instead.
  • Writing or rewriting your essay. Your essay must be your own work. AI-generated text submitted as your own writing violates the academic integrity policy.
  • Summarizing or paraphrasing sources you haven't read. AI cannot accurately represent the contents of a specific scholarly article — it will hallucinate the argument.
  • Checking whether a source exists or is credible. AI cannot verify sources. Only a library database search can confirm a source is real and accurately described.
  • Generating or translating poetry quotes. AI frequently misquotes poems, conflates different poems, or invents lines. Always quote directly from the actual text.

How to Prompt AI Productively for Research

Here are examples of prompts that use AI appropriately as a research companion, and what each one gives you:

Keyword Generation
I'm writing a literary analysis essay about Anne Sexton's poem "Her Kind." What search terms and keywords would be most useful for finding scholarly literary criticism about this poem in an academic database?
What this gives you: A list of terms — confession, confessional poetry, female persona, feminist reading, identity, self-construction, witch figure — that you can test in the library database. You're using AI to do terminology brainstorming, not to find sources.
Interpretive Brainstorming
I'm writing about Pablo Neruda's "Sonnet XVII." I've noticed that the poem describes love in terms of what it is not rather than what it is. What interpretive angles might I take when analyzing how Neruda uses imagery to develop this idea?
What this gives you: A list of possible angles — negative theology, apophatic language, the limits of representation, the ineffability of intimacy — that you can explore in your own close reading and then look for in scholarship. AI is helping you think, not thinking for you.
Argument Testing
My thesis is that Komunyakaa uses reflective surfaces in "Facing It" to suggest that trauma cannot be separated from identity. What are the strongest objections to this argument that I should be prepared to address?
What this gives you: Potential counterarguments — the poem might be more about public vs. private memory than individual identity; the reflection could be read as erasure rather than merging; etc. You're stress-testing your argument before you write it, not outsourcing the writing itself.

Formative Check — Think First, Then Reveal
1You're having trouble finding library database sources on Emily Dickinson's "Because I could not stop for Death" and decide to ask ChatGPT for suggestions. What specifically should — and should you not — ask it for?
Answer

Ask AI for: keywords, related critical terms ("carpe diem," "psychopomp," "thanatopsis," "eschatology"), names of scholars known to work on Dickinson (you can then search for them in the database), and subject headings that library databases might use. Do not ask AI for: specific article titles, author names with citations, or any source recommendation you intend to use in your paper. Everything AI tells you in response to those requests needs to be independently verified in a library database before you act on it — and at that point, you might as well just search the database directly.

2You ask an AI tool to explain what "confessional poetry" means because you don't understand the term in an article you're reading. Is this an appropriate use of AI? Why or why not?
Answer

Yes, this is an appropriate use. Explaining established critical terminology is something AI tools do reasonably well — "confessional poetry" is well documented in their training data, and a general explanation of the term helps you read the scholarly article you already have. What you should not do is ask AI to summarize the article's argument for you or to find you additional articles on confessional poetry — those would be inappropriate uses. Using AI to understand vocabulary is analogous to looking up a word in a dictionary: it's a tool for comprehension, not a source of scholarly authority.

3A friend tells you they asked ChatGPT to "write a paragraph analyzing the imagery in Neruda's Sonnet XVII" and then revised it to sound more like their own writing. Is this an appropriate use of AI for this assignment?
Answer

No. The assignment requires original literary analysis — the ability to read a poem closely and develop your own interpretation of it. A paragraph generated by AI and then lightly revised is still fundamentally AI-generated content, not the student's own analysis. This violates the academic integrity policy regardless of how much revision was done, because the thinking — the interpretive work — was outsourced. Additionally, AI frequently misreads or misquotes poems, meaning the AI-generated analysis may contain errors that the student then inadvertently perpetuates.

The Non-Negotiable Principle

The Verification Principle

All of the appropriate uses of AI in research share a common feature: they produce output that you then verify or act on independently. AI suggestions for keywords only become useful when you test them in a library database. AI-generated background information only becomes trustworthy when you cross-check key facts against a reliable source. AI-proposed counterarguments are only useful when you evaluate them against your own reading of the poem.

This is the verification principle: never use AI output in your academic work without independently confirming that it is accurate. This applies to citations, to facts, to quotes, and to any specific claim. If you can't verify it, don't use it.

How to Verify an AI-Generated Citation

If you've asked AI for source suggestions (which is generally not recommended, but may happen), here is the only acceptable way to proceed:

1
Take the author name and article title to a library database

Search the MLA International Bibliography, Academic Search Complete, or JSTOR for the exact author name and article title. Do not assume the article exists because the journal name sounds real.

2
Confirm every detail of the citation

When you find the article in the database, confirm that the volume number, issue number, year, and page range match what the AI gave you. AI frequently gets these details wrong even when the article itself is real.

3
Read the actual article before citing it

Do not cite an article you haven't read. AI summaries of scholarly articles are frequently inaccurate — the AI may have described an argument the article never makes, attributed a claim to the wrong author, or simply invented the article's contents. You must read it to know what it actually says.

4
If you can't verify the source, don't use it

If a library database search returns nothing for the author and title the AI gave you, the source does not exist — full stop. Do not use it, and do not try to find a "similar" source to substitute for it. Return to your database and search for a real source using appropriate search terms.

A Final Note on AI Transparency

The academic integrity statement for this course specifies that AI tools may be used for specific purposes when the instructor approves, and that any use must be acknowledged and cited. If you use an AI tool to help generate search keywords or brainstorm essay angles, a brief note in your process documentation is appropriate. What you may not do is use AI to write, substantively revise, or generate sources for work that you submit as your own — or submit any AI output that you haven't independently verified.

When in doubt, ask. It is always better to ask your instructor whether a particular use of AI is appropriate than to assume and be wrong.
