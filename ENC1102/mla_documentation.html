🔍 MLA Documentation: Quoting from Your Secondary Sources (Direct Quotes)

Remember the Literary Research Essay Research Assignment? Where you located your secondary sources, saved their bibliographic information, identified their authors, and determined whether they were in .html or .pdf format?

Good — because you're going to need some of that information now.

Creating direct quotes from your secondary sources still requires the same three basic parts: a signal phrase, a quote, and a parenthetical citation. The format changes slightly depending on (1) whether the article is a PDF with page numbers, or an HTML article without them, and (2) whether you place the author's name in the signal phrase or in the parenthetical citation.

The Four Scenarios — All Four Possibilities with Samples

Scenario A — PDF + Author in Signal Phrase

Literary critic Valerie Boyd argues that Hurston uses Delia's labor as "the central site of her moral and economic independence" (Boyd 114).

Author named in signal phrase → only page number needed in parenthetical. PDF articles have page numbers.
Scenario B — PDF + Author in Parenthetical

In the story, Delia's labor is described by scholars as "the central site of her moral and economic independence" (Boyd 114).

Author not named in signal phrase → author's last name + page number in parenthetical. Note: Scenario A is generally stronger — naming the scholar signals authority.
Scenario C — HTML + Author in Signal Phrase

Scholar Aidan Day contends that Carter's wolf transformation represents "the collapse of the boundary between civilized and animal selfhood" (Day).

HTML articles have no page numbers → parenthetical contains only the author's last name. If author already named in signal phrase, parenthetical may be omitted.
Scenario D — HTML + Author in Parenthetical

Carter's wolf transformation has been read as "the collapse of the boundary between civilized and animal selfhood" (Day).

Author not named in signal phrase and article is HTML → parenthetical contains only the author's last name. No page number because none exists.
💬 Introducing Your Secondary Sources — Signal Phrase Tips

Secondary source quotes carry more weight when students introduce them with signal phrases that name the scholar and establish their authority — rather than generic openers like "According to an article" or, worse, no introduction at all. Compare the two approaches:

✅ Stronger: Name the Scholar

Literary critic Debra Shostak argues that Ozzie's rooftop confrontation "transforms personal humiliation into a communal act of moral reckoning" (Shostak 88).

Naming the scholar and describing their role signals to readers that this is an expert perspective, not just someone's opinion.

⚠️ Weaker: Anonymous Introduction

According to an article, Ozzie's rooftop confrontation "transforms personal humiliation into a communal act of moral reckoning" (Shostak 88).

This doesn't tell readers who is making the argument or why they're worth listening to. Save the author's name for the signal phrase — don't hide it in the parenthetical.

📋 Signal Verb Bank — Vary These Across Your Essay

Making a claim:
argues • asserts
claims • contends
maintains • insists
Explaining or describing:
explains • notes
observes • describes
points out • states
Analyzing or interpreting:
analyzes • interprets
examines • reads
suggests • reveals
Emphasizing:
emphasizes • highlights
stresses • underscores
demonstrates • shows
💡 Use present tense with these verbs when introducing quotes: "Shostak argues" — not "Shostak argued." Academic writing convention treats published scholarly work as always in the present.
✏️ MLA Documentation: Paraphrasing Your Secondary Sources

Uh, oh.

And now you get to learn about one of the most frequent reasons innocent, big-hearted students like yourself get accused by their teachers of plagiarism — which, as you now know from the previous page, is the act of incorporating the ideas or language of another person without properly acknowledging and/or documenting it.

Paraphrasing is a popular and effective technique for incorporating ideas from a source into your essay. Unlike direct quotes, paraphrases aren't word-for-word quotations. When paraphrasing, you're incorporating an author's idea — but completely rewriting it in your own words. Why bother? Because it's an efficient method for getting complex ideas into your paragraphs without needing lengthy, sometimes cumbersome, direct quotes.

Paraphrases are a bit trickier to execute than direct quotes, though, and the consequences of messing them up can occasionally be serious.

⚠️ What Paraphrasing Actually Means — and the Mistake That Gets Students in Trouble

The most common paraphrasing error — and the one most likely to result in a plagiarism flag — is Find and Replace Plagiarism: swapping individual words for synonyms while keeping the original sentence structure completely intact. This is not a paraphrase. It's just a disguised copy.

A true paraphrase requires you to fully absorb the scholar's idea, close the article, and then rewrite the idea from scratch in your own sentence structure and phrasing — with only the core idea borrowed, not the wording. Here's what that difference looks like:

Example Using Hurston's "Sweat"

Original Passage (from the scholarly article):

"Hurston constructs the washtub as a potent symbol of Delia's labor and autonomy: through her work, Delia has earned both her home and her independence, making Sykes's attempt to remove it an act of direct symbolic violence against her selfhood."

— Boyd, "Domestic Space and Identity in Hurston's 'Sweat'" (example)
❌ Find and Replace Plagiarism — Not Acceptable
Hurston builds the washtub as a powerful representation of Delia's toil and freedom: through her efforts, Delia has gained both her home and her self-determination, making Sykes's effort to take it an act of direct symbolic aggression against her identity (Boyd 114).
Words swapped for synonyms are highlighted above. The sentence structure is identical to the original — this is still plagiarism, even with a citation attached. The citation doesn't fix the problem.
✅ True Paraphrase — Acceptable
According to Boyd, the washtub in the story carries significant symbolic weight: it stands for the years of hard work that gave Delia ownership of her own life and home. When Sykes threatens to take it away, he is attacking not just an object but her right to exist as an independent person (114).
The sentence structure is entirely different from the original. The core idea is preserved and cited — but the wording, syntax, and sentence organization are all the student's own.

Example Using Carter's "The Company of Wolves"

Original Passage (from the scholarly article):

"Carter's girl does not submit to the wolf's dominance but instead reclaims the encounter on her own terms, transforming what begins as predatory pursuit into an act of willful, self-determined exchange."

— Day, "Desire, Danger, and Self-Making in Carter's 'The Company of Wolves'" (example)
❌ Find and Replace Plagiarism — Not Acceptable
Carter's girl does not give in to the wolf's control but instead takes back the encounter on her own conditions, changing what begins as hunting pursuit into an act of deliberate, self-chosen exchange (Day).
Same structure, just different words. This is not a paraphrase — it's still plagiarism.
✅ True Paraphrase — Acceptable
Day reads the girl's final exchange with the wolf not as defeat but as an assertion of agency — she refuses the role of victim and instead steers the encounter toward a conclusion of her own choosing (Day).
Entirely different sentence structure and phrasing. The scholar's core idea is preserved, credited, and expressed in the student's own voice.

🔁 A Simple Three-Step Method for Paraphrasing

Step 1
Read & Understand

Read the passage carefully until you genuinely understand what the scholar is arguing. Don't start writing yet.

Step 2
Close the Article

Scroll away from or minimize the passage. Write your paraphrase without looking at the original. If you have to peek at it to write, you're copying.

Step 3
Check & Cite

Re-open the original and compare your version. If any phrases match, rewrite them. Then add your signal phrase and parenthetical citation — paraphrases still require full MLA documentation.
