Stage Four

Two Different Tasks, Done in Order

Editing and proofreading are the final stage of the writing process, and the ones most directly connected to the Sentence Structure, Grammar/Punctuation/Mechanics, and Vocabulary criteria on the grading rubric. Though they are often treated as one thing, they are genuinely different tasks that work best when done separately and in order: editing first, proofreading second. For a research essay, each stage has an additional layer — checking how your sources are being used and whether they are documented correctly.

✏️ Editing: Style & Clarity

Improving how your sentences are written. Are they clear? Concise? Well-constructed? For research essays, editing also means assessing whether each source is working the way it should and whether your own analytical voice is still leading the argument.

🔍 Proofreading: Errors & Mechanics

Catching mechanical mistakes: grammar, spelling, punctuation, capitalization, and MLA formatting. For research essays, this includes verifying that every in-text citation is accurate and that every source cited in the essay has a properly formatted Works Cited entry.

Stage Four · Part One

Editing Your Essay

Editing a research essay works at two levels simultaneously: the sentence level — are my sentences clear, precise, and varied? — and the source level — are my sources integrated effectively and appropriately? Work through both passes before moving on to proofreading.

Sentence-Level Editing
  • Is every sentence clear and direct — no unnecessary words?
  • Are you using the specific vocabulary of literary analysis? (imagery, connotation, figurative language, etc.)
  • Are sentences varied in structure, or does every one begin the same way?
  • Is each body paragraph's topic sentence specific enough to tell the reader exactly what the paragraph argues?
  • Do transitions clearly signal the relationship between ideas — within paragraphs and between them?
  • Is every poem and poet discussed in the present tense? (Plath "uses" — not "used")
Source-Level Editing
  • Is each source — poem quote or secondary source — introduced with a signal phrase naming the author?
  • Does your own analysis follow every quote? Both poem quotes and secondary source passages need to be analyzed, not just dropped in.
  • Are you quoting too heavily? Your analytical voice should lead the argument; sources support it — not the other way around.
  • Are any secondary source quotes too long? For an essay of this length, keep direct quotes to three typed lines or fewer.
  • Does each source actually support the analytical point of its paragraph, or is it loosely related?
  • Did you meet the Module Seven requirements: one direct quote, one paraphrase, and one summary from three separate sources?
Literary Present Tense — Poetry Essays

When analyzing a poem, always use the present tense. The poem exists on the page right now; its speaker speaks, its images appear, its metaphors work — in the present tense, not the past. This convention applies to both the poem itself and to what critics and scholars argue.

Incorrect Correct
Plath compared the baby to a watch. Plath compares the baby to a watch.
The speaker denied any connection to her child. The speaker denies any connection to her child.
Anderson argued that the simile strips the image of warmth. Anderson argues that the simile strips the image of warmth.
💬 Vary Your Signal Phrases

A signal phrase introduces a quote, paraphrase, or summary by naming the author and characterizing what they do. Relying on "According to [author]…" for every source is repetitive and weakens your analytical voice. Use the options below — all in the present tense — to vary your introductions and characterize each source's stance accurately.

Neutral / Descriptive
  • notes
  • explains
  • observes
  • writes
  • points out
  • describes
  • traces
Analytical / Argumentative
  • argues
  • contends
  • asserts
  • claims
  • maintains
  • insists
  • proposes
Interpretive
  • suggests
  • implies
  • demonstrates
  • examines
  • concludes
  • illuminates
  • reveals
Contrasting / Qualifying
  • acknowledges
  • concedes
  • qualifies
  • complicates
  • challenges
  • nuances
  • reconsiders

Stage Four · Part Two

Proofreading Your Essay

Proofreading catches the errors editing leaves behind: grammar, spelling, punctuation, capitalization, and MLA formatting. For a research essay, verifying the MLA documentation is substantial enough to be its own dedicated pass — a citation audit — completed before you check grammar and mechanics. Work through the steps below in order.

📋 The Three-Step Citation Audit
Step 1
Match every in-text citation to a Works Cited entry

Find every in-text citation in your essay — whether the author appears in the signal phrase or in a parenthetical — and confirm that the same author has an entry on your Works Cited page. The rule works in both directions: if a source is cited in the essay, it must have a Works Cited entry; if a source has a Works Cited entry, it must appear at least once in the essay. Your Works Cited should have exactly four entries: the poem and your three secondary sources.

Step 2
Verify that paraphrases and summaries are attributed

Students reliably cite direct quotes but sometimes forget that paraphrases (a source's idea restated in your own words) and summaries (an overview of a source's overall argument) also require attribution. Read through your essay for any sentences clearly based on a source's ideas that don't identify the author either in the signal phrase or in a parenthetical citation, and correct them. Uncited paraphrases and summaries constitute plagiarism.

Step 3
Review the Works Cited page formatting

Confirm the Works Cited page is correctly formatted: alphabetical order by author's last name; double-spaced throughout with no extra space between entries; hanging indent on each entry (first line flush left, all subsequent lines indented ½"); the words Works Cited centered at the top in regular — not bold — font; and all URLs in plain black text with no color or underline.

Poetry-Specific MLA Format Checks

In addition to the standard citation audit, check the following rules specific to poetry essays before you submit:

  • Line numbers, not page numbers. Parenthetical citations for the poem cite line numbers: (lines 1–4) on first use, (5–8) on all subsequent uses.
  • Slash notation for short quotes. When a poem quotation of three or fewer lines is run into your prose, forward slashes with a space on each side mark the line breaks: "Love set you going like a fat gold watch. / The midwife slapped your footsoles" (lines 1–2).
  • Block quotes for four or more lines. If you quote four or more consecutive lines, use a block quote: start a new line, indent the passage one inch from the left margin, and omit quotation marks. No block quotes were required for this essay since all poem quotes were kept to three lines or fewer.
  • Poem title in quotation marks. Poem titles always go in quotation marks — not italics — both in the essay text and on the Works Cited page.
  • No parenthetical for HTML sources with author in signal phrase. When a secondary source has no page numbers (HTML format) and you have named the author in your signal phrase, there is no parenthetical citation at all. The absence of a citation is not an error; it is the correct format.
Grammar & Mechanics Checklist

After completing the citation audit, do one final read for sentence-level errors. The three most serious errors in academic writing are:

  • Sentence fragments — incomplete sentences that lack a subject, a verb, or both.
  • Comma splices — two complete sentences joined only with a comma, with no coordinating conjunction.
  • Run-on sentences — two or more complete sentences run together without adequate punctuation or a conjunction.

Check also for consistent use of literary present tense throughout, proper capitalization of authors' names and poem titles, and correct spelling of poets' names (Komunyakaa, Neruda, Espada — these are easy to misspell).
