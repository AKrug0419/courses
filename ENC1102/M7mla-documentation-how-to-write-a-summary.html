MLA Documentation · Module Seven

How to Write
a Summary

Condensing a scholar's argument into your own words — and how to cite it correctly in MLA format.

Section One

What Is a Summary?

A summary is a condensed restatement — in your own words — of the main argument or overall point of a source or a large section of one. Unlike a direct quote or a paraphrase, a summary does not focus on the specific language or details of a passage. Instead, it steps back and captures the big picture: what is this scholar's central claim?

Like a paraphrase, a summary must be written entirely in your own words and must still acknowledge the source through a signal phrase, a citation, or both. Summarizing someone else's argument without attribution is plagiarism — the idea remains theirs even when you compress it.

The Key Distinction

The most important thing to understand about summaries is how they differ from paraphrases. A paraphrase zooms in — it restates a specific passage in your own words, staying close to the original in scope and often in length. A summary zooms out — it compresses a larger argument, section, or article into a single sentence or two, focusing only on the main claim and leaving out the supporting details.

❝
Direct Quote

You reproduce the scholar's exact words, enclosed in quotation marks.

Use when

  • The scholar's exact wording is striking, precise, or will be the subject of your own analysis
  • The phrasing is so specific that paraphrasing would distort it
  • You want to introduce a term the scholar coins
Scope: a specific phrase or sentence
✎
Paraphrase

You restate a specific passage completely in your own words, preserving the original's level of detail.

Use when

  • The scholar's idea matters more than their specific wording
  • The original passage is complex and clearer in your own words
  • You want to vary your evidence style within a paragraph
Scope: a specific sentence or passage — similar in length to the original
◈
Summary

You condense a scholar's overall argument or main point into one or two sentences. Details are left out.

Use when

  • You need to mention a scholar's general position without diving into specifics
  • A source's conclusion matters to your argument but its evidence does not
  • You want to gesture at a larger conversation in the field without stopping to quote it
Scope: the whole article, a major section, or the scholar's main thesis — always shorter than the original

Formative Check

Which type of source use fits this situation?

A literary critic publishes a ten-page article arguing that Yusef Komunyakaa's use of the reflective surface in "Facing It" forces the speaker into an unstable position between grief and survival. You want to mention this argument in your body paragraph — not the critic's specific wording or detailed evidence, just the main claim — because it supports the point you're making about the poem's central tension.

Answer

Summary. You don't need the critic's exact words (which would require a direct quote), and you don't need to restate the details of a specific passage (which would require a paraphrase). You just need to convey the scholar's central claim — which is exactly what a summary does. A one-sentence summary lets you bring in the scholar's authority without slowing your paragraph down with detail it doesn't need.

In her study of Komunyakaa's Vietnam poetry, Andrea Chen argues that the poem's reflective surface positions the speaker in a state of continuous oscillation between survival and mourning (45).
✦

Section Two

How to Write a Summary

A reliable summary does not happen by skimming the source and copying its topic sentences. The process requires you to understand the scholar's argument well enough to express it in your own language. Follow these five steps.

1

Read the source all the way through

Do not try to write a summary from an incomplete reading. Read the entire article — or the section you're summarizing — carefully before you write anything. Pay attention to the thesis statement (usually near the beginning), the topic sentence of each paragraph, and the conclusion, which often restates the main argument in a different form.

For your essay: Your two internet sources may be long. You don't have to memorize every detail — but you do need to understand what the scholar is ultimately arguing before you can represent it fairly.
2

Set the source aside and identify the main claim

Close the article (or look away from it) and ask yourself: "In one sentence, what is this scholar's argument?" Forcing yourself to answer without looking at the text is the most reliable way to produce a genuine summary rather than a disguised paraphrase. If you can't answer the question, read the article again.

3

Write your sentence — without looking at the original

Draft your one- or two-sentence summary entirely in your own words. Do not look at the source while you write. The phrasing must be yours — not the scholar's words rearranged, not their sentence structure with synonyms substituted. Proper nouns (the author's name, the poem's title) may remain as-is.

Watch for patchwriting: If your summary sentence still sounds like the original — same structure, same key phrases — you have produced a paraphrase at best, and patchwriting at worst. Set the original aside and try again.
4

Check your sentence against the original

Now look back at the source. Ask two questions: (1) Is my summary accurate? Does it represent what the scholar actually argues, or have I distorted, oversimplified, or exaggerated their position? (2) Is my summary specific enough? A summary that says only "Smith discusses Brooks's poem" tells the reader nothing. A useful summary identifies the scholar's actual claim.

5

Add your signal phrase and citation

Introduce your summary with a signal phrase that names the author (or provides context) and uses a present-tense signal verb. Then add the appropriate parenthetical citation based on your source type. See Section Three for the citation formats.

Signal verbs for summaries (use present tense): argues, contends, maintains, claims, traces, examines, surveys, explores, demonstrates, proposes, suggests, reads, interprets, situates.
✦

Section Three

Citation Rules for Summaries

The citation format for a summary is identical to that for a direct quote or paraphrase — the scholar still receives credit through a signal phrase, a parenthetical citation, or both. The format depends on your source type (PDF with fixed page numbers vs. HTML without page numbers) and where the author's name appears in your sentence.

PDF Source Fixed Page Numbers

✓  Pattern A — Author's Name in the Citation

Summary sentence (LastName PageNumber).

Critics have argued that the reflective surface of the Vietnam Veterans Memorial functions as Komunyakaa's central metaphor, staging a continuous collapse of the boundary between the living and the dead (Chen 45).

Author not named in signal → include last name + page number in the parenthetical. No comma between them.

✓  Pattern B — Author's Name in the Signal Phrase

Author argues that summary sentence (PageNumber).

In her study of Komunyakaa's Vietnam poetry, Andrea Chen argues that the memorial's reflective surface forces the speaker into an unstable position — simultaneously survivor and ghost — that defines the poem's psychological landscape (45).

Author named in signal → page number only in the parenthetical. Do not repeat the name.

No comma between the author's name and the page number in MLA format: (Chen 45) not (Chen, 45).
HTML / Internet Source No Fixed Page Numbers

✓  Pattern C — Author's Name in the Citation

Summary sentence (LastName).

Some critics have read Sexton's identification with female outcasts in "Her Kind" as a move that goes beyond metaphor — the speaker does not merely compare herself to these figures but claims their identity as her own, giving the poem its confrontational intensity (Webb).

HTML source has no page numbers. Author not named in signal → last name only in the parenthetical. Do not invent a page number.

✓  Pattern D — Author's Name in the Signal Phrase

Author argues that summary sentence.

Critic Marcus Webb argues that what distinguishes Sexton's use of the witch and outcast archetypes in "Her Kind" is the speaker's act of identification — not encounter but belonging — which is what gives the poem its lasting challenge to the reader's sympathy.

HTML source + author named in signal → no parenthetical citation needed. The period goes after the last word of your sentence (or inside the closing quotation mark if you end with a quote). Do not add a redundant (Webb) at the end.

When the author is in the signal phrase and the source has no page numbers, no parenthetical citation is required. The reader can find the source on your Works Cited page.
No Identified Author Use Shortened Title

✓  HTML — No Author

Summary sentence ("Shortened Title").

One recent analysis of Espada's poem notes that its most distinctive feature is its refusal to frame the September 11 victims as national symbols, insisting instead on honoring them as undocumented immigrant workers whose labor had been largely invisible ("Alabanza and the Politics").

No author identified → use the first two to three significant words of the title in quotation marks. HTML source: no page number.

✓  PDF — No Author

Summary sentence ("Shortened Title" PageNumber).

A survey of critical responses to Neruda's "Sonnet XVII" notes that the poem has consistently resisted allegorical readings because its imagery is so deliberately vague that it refuses to resolve into any single interpretation ("Neruda's Metaphors" 12).

No author + PDF source → title fragment in quotes + page number. Use the database's Citation Tool to confirm pagination.

Sources without identified authors are less credible than named scholarship. When possible, choose sources with identifiable authors for your research essay.

Formative Check

Which citation format is correct for this situation?

You are summarizing an argument from a literary criticism website — an HTML source with no fixed page numbers — written by critic Sarah Donnelly. You have already named her in your signal phrase: "Critic Sarah Donnelly argues that Brooks's use of the second person in 'the mother'..." How does your sentence end, and do you include a parenthetical citation?

Answer

No parenthetical citation is needed. Because you've named the author in the signal phrase and the source is an HTML article with no page numbers, Pattern D applies: the sentence ends with a period (or with the period after the closing quotation mark, if you end with quoted language), and no parenthetical appears. Adding (Donnelly) at the end would be redundant — the reader already knows who you're citing.

Critic Sarah Donnelly argues that Brooks's use of the second person address in "the mother" forces the reader into an uncomfortable position of being both witness and recipient of the speaker's grief.

Compare this to a situation where the author is not in the signal phrase, where (Donnelly) would appear at the end before the period.

Section Four

Complete Summary Examples

Each example below shows a source passage followed by a correctly written summary. Notice how the summary captures the scholar's main argument in a single sentence — without reproducing their wording, following their sentence structure, or including their supporting details.

Example 1 — PDF Source, Author in Signal Phrase

Source type: PDF · Fixed Pages Author location: Signal Phrase

Original Passage

"The Vietnam Veterans Memorial's black granite surface serves Komunyakaa not merely as a backdrop but as the poem's central figure: a mirror that refuses to distinguish between the living and the dead, between the soldier who survived and those whose names are carved permanently into stone. Komunyakaa exploits this ambiguity throughout 'Facing It,' using the reflective surface to stage a continuous oscillation between presence and absence, self and other, past and present. The speaker's face appears and disappears within the stone — at once a visitor to the memorial and, in moments of reflection, one of the dead — and this instability defines what it means to carry the weight of survival."— Andrea Chen, "The Reflective Self: Memory and the Monumental in Komunyakaa's Vietnam Poetry," MELUS, p. 45.

→

Summary (Written by You)

In her study of Komunyakaa's Vietnam poetry, Andrea Chen argues that the memorial's reflective surface functions as the poem's central metaphor — a mirror that collapses the boundary between survivor and the dead, forcing the speaker to occupy both positions simultaneously (45).

What the summary does: Identifies the scholar, uses a present-tense signal verb ("argues"), and captures the central claim — the mirror as a metaphor for unstable identity — in one sentence, without reproducing Chen's phrasing or including the paragraph's supporting observations. Pattern B: author in signal phrase → page number only in citation.

Example 2 — HTML Source, Author in Parenthetical Citation

Source type: HTML · No Page Numbers Author location: Parenthetical

Original Passage

"Sexton's 'Her Kind' is perhaps the most sustained articulation of what we might call the 'witch's poetics' in the confessional tradition — a mode of writing that refuses to separate the speaker's identity from society's most stigmatized categories. Where Plath's witch-figures tend to be transient presences, apparitions at the edges of the poem, Sexton makes the witch central and continuous: the speaker does not merely encounter these figures; she claims them as her own face in the mirror, her own voice, her own kind. This move from encounter to identification is what gives the poem its unusual intensity and its lasting challenge to the reader's sympathy."— Marcus Webb, "Confessional Defiance: Anne Sexton and the Female Outcast," Plath Profiles [online, no page numbers].

→

Summary (Written by You)

Some critics have read Sexton's use of outcast archetypes in "Her Kind" not as mere comparison but as a sustained act of self-identification — the speaker does not describe these figures from the outside but claims their identity as her own, which is the source of the poem's confrontational energy (Webb).

What the summary does: Accurately captures Webb's central claim — that Sexton moves from encounter to identification — without using Webb's specific phrases ("witch's poetics," "transient presences") or following his sentence structure. Pattern C: HTML source, author not in signal → last name only in citation, no page number.

Example 3 — HTML Source, Author in Signal Phrase (No Parenthetical Needed)

Source type: HTML · No Page Numbers Author location: Signal Phrase

Original Passage

"Espada's poem is unusual among the many elegies written about September 11 in that it refuses the nationalist frame most such poems adopt. Rather than mourning the victims as Americans lost in a national tragedy, 'Alabanza' insists on honoring them specifically as immigrant workers — undocumented, underpaid, and largely invisible to the culture that employed them. The poem's repeated 'alabanza' (praise) functions as a liturgical refrain that transforms mourning into a counter-cultural act of recognition."— Diana Fuss, "Labor, Elegy, and the Politics of Praise," Contemporary Literature [online, no page numbers].

→

Summary (Written by You)

Critic Diana Fuss argues that what distinguishes "Alabanza" from other September 11 elegies is its insistence on honoring the victims not as national symbols but as immigrant laborers — a distinction the poem reinforces through its repeated "alabanza" refrain, which she reads as an act of counter-cultural recognition.

What the summary does: Author named in signal phrase ("Diana Fuss argues") + HTML source = no parenthetical citation at all. The sentence ends with a period. The reader can locate the source on the Works Cited page under "Fuss." Note also: the summary includes the word "alabanza" in quotation marks because it is the poem's actual title and the central term — this is not copying the scholar's language but referring to the poem itself.

Section Five

Common Errors

Error 1 — Over-Detailing: The "Summary" That Is Actually a Paraphrase

The most common mistake is writing a summary that includes too much of the original's supporting detail. If your sentence follows the scholar's argument step by step, reproducing their reasoning in condensed form, you have written a paraphrase — not a summary. A summary should be shorter than a paraphrase and should leave out the specific evidence and reasoning, keeping only the main claim.

✗  Over-Detailed — This is a paraphrase, not a summary

In her analysis, Chen argues that Komunyakaa uses the memorial's reflective surface as a central figure, exploiting its ambiguity to stage a continuous oscillation between presence and absence, between self and other, so that the speaker's face appears and disappears within the stone, positioning him as both a surviving visitor and, in moments of reflection, one of the dead, which Chen suggests defines what it means to carry the weight of survival (45).

Problem: This sentence follows Chen's paragraph almost point-by-point, reproducing her specific examples ("oscillation between presence and absence," "face appears and disappears") and her conclusion. It is a compressed paraphrase, not a summary.

✓  Correct — Main claim only

In her study of Komunyakaa's Vietnam poetry, Andrea Chen argues that the memorial's reflective surface functions as the poem's central metaphor — a mirror that collapses the boundary between survivor and the dead, forcing the speaker to occupy both positions simultaneously (45).

Why it works: The sentence captures Chen's thesis in original language without following her reasoning step by step. The supporting details ("oscillation," "face appears and disappears") are omitted because they are the evidence for the claim, not the claim itself.

Error 2 — The Empty Summary: Too Vague to Be Useful

A summary that says only that the scholar "discusses" or "talks about" a topic is useless — it doesn't tell the reader what the scholar actually argues. Identify the scholar's specific claim, not just their subject.

✗  Too vague — tells the reader nothing

In her article, Andrea Chen discusses Komunyakaa's "Facing It" and the Vietnam Veterans Memorial (45).

Problem: This tells us only the topic, not the argument. What does Chen say about the poem? What is her claim? A useful summary answers that question.

✓  Specific — captures the actual argument

In her study of Komunyakaa's Vietnam poetry, Andrea Chen argues that the memorial's reflective surface functions as the poem's central metaphor — a mirror that collapses the boundary between survivor and the dead, forcing the speaker to occupy both positions simultaneously (45).

Why it works: The reader now knows what Chen argues, not just that she wrote about the poem. This summary contributes to the paragraph's argument rather than merely name-dropping a source.

Error 3 — Missing Attribution: The Unacknowledged Summary

A summary without a signal phrase or citation looks like your own original idea — which means you are presenting someone else's argument as your own. This is plagiarism, even though you didn't quote directly. Every summary needs attribution.

✗  No attribution — plagiarism

The memorial's reflective surface functions as Komunyakaa's central metaphor, creating an unstable space in which the speaker simultaneously occupies the roles of survivor and ghost.

Problem: This is Chen's argument, but there is no signal phrase and no citation. The reader has no way to know this came from a source. This is plagiarism.

✓  Properly attributed

In her study of Komunyakaa's Vietnam poetry, Andrea Chen argues that the memorial's reflective surface functions as the poem's central metaphor — a mirror that forces the speaker to occupy the roles of survivor and ghost simultaneously (45).

Why it works: The signal phrase ("Andrea Chen argues") and the citation (45) make clear that this argument belongs to Chen, not to you.

Formative Check

Identify the error in this "summary" of a critic's argument about Pablo Neruda's "Sonnet XVII."

In his article, critic Jason Moore argues that Neruda's "Sonnet XVII" works to define love by using a series of unusual comparisons, saying that the speaker describes love not as "a rose of salt" or as "an obscure arrow" — both being conventional poetic images of desire — but as something more intimate and invisible: "the secret word" and "the hidden light," images drawn from natural phenomena that resist easy interpretation and suggest a love that is felt rather than seen or described in traditional lyric terms (Moore 78).

Answer

This is a paraphrase, not a summary. The sentence follows Moore's argument in detail — listing his specific examples (the rejected comparisons, the alternative images), reproducing his interpretive logic step by step, and concluding with his specific reading ("a love that is felt rather than seen"). It is far more detailed than a summary needs to be and is essentially an over-detailed paraphrase.

A summary of Moore's argument would capture only his central claim in one or two sentences, leaving out the specific images and reasoning:

Critic Jason Moore argues that "Sonnet XVII" defines love through a series of negative comparisons — by rejecting conventional poetic images of desire in favor of more elusive, intimate alternatives — a strategy that suggests love in the poem is experienced rather than articulated (78).
