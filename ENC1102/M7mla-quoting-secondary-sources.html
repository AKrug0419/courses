MLA Documentation · Module Seven

Quoting from
Secondary Sources

How to quote from scholarly articles: signal phrases, the four citation patterns, and the most common errors to avoid.

Secondary sources, the scholarly articles and web reviews you bring into your essay alongside the poem itself, follow a small set of conventions when you quote from them. The conventions depend on two simple variables: the source format (PDF article with fixed page numbers, or HTML article without them) and where you place the author's name (in the signal phrase, or inside the parenthetical citation). Together those variables produce the four patterns you will use throughout your essay. The second half of the page covers the most common quoting errors to watch for during proofreading.

Part One Core Skills

Section One

Signal Phrases for Secondary Sources

A signal phrase introduces a quotation by naming the author or providing context before the quoted words appear. For secondary sources, strong signal phrases establish the scholar's identity and authority. It is generally preferable to name the scholar in the signal phrase rather than hiding their name inside the parenthetical citation. Naming the scholar gives them credit explicitly and makes the sentence read more naturally. Alternate between placing the name in the signal phrase and placing it in the parenthetical so that your paragraphs do not become repetitive.

Signal verbs for secondary sources, vary these throughout your essay:

arguesclaimscontendsmaintainssuggestsobservesnotesexplainswritespoints outconcludesassertsdemonstratesshows
Present Tense, Always

Always use present tense for signal verbs with literary criticism: “Westling argues,” not “Westling argued.” Scholars' arguments remain active in the present, even if the article was published decades ago.

Two patterns, alternate between them

✓ Author named in signal phrase

Literary critic Louise Westling argues that “Hulga's adoption of nihilism functions as a defense mechanism rather than a genuine philosophical position” (48).

Naming the scholar and identifying their field establishes their authority and makes the source feel purposeful rather than decorative.

✓ Author named in parenthetical instead

Scholars of O'Connor have noted that Hulga's adoption of nihilism “functions as a defense mechanism rather than a genuine philosophical position” (Westling 48).

Name omitted from signal phrase, so it must appear in the parenthetical citation before the page number.

Section Two

Pattern A: PDF Article, Author's Name in the Citation

When your secondary source is a PDF article (fixed page numbers) and you have not named the author in the signal phrase, include both the author's last name and the page number inside the parenthetical citation. No comma separates the name from the number. The period falls after the closing parenthesis.

Pattern A Format: “quotation” (LastName PageNumber).
✗ Comma between name and page

Scholars of O'Connor have noted that Hulga's nihilism “functions as a defense mechanism rather than a genuine philosophical position” (Westling, 48).

Never place a comma between the author's name and the page number in MLA format.

✓ Correct: no comma

Scholars of O'Connor have noted that Hulga's nihilism “functions as a defense mechanism rather than a genuine philosophical position” (Westling 48).

Last name and page number, separated only by a single space.

Two Author Sources

If a PDF article has two authors, use both last names connected with “and”: (Lawrence and Jewett 29).

Section Three

Pattern B: PDF Article, Author's Name in the Signal Phrase

When your secondary source is a PDF article and you have already named the author in the signal phrase, the parenthetical citation contains the page number only. The author's name is not repeated inside the parentheses.

Pattern B Format: Author argues that “quotation” (PageNumber).
✗ Name repeated in citation

Literary critic Louise Westling argues that “Hulga's adoption of nihilism functions as a defense mechanism rather than a genuine philosophical position” (Westling 48).

Author named in signal phrase, so the name should not be repeated. Page number only.

✓ Correct: page number only

Literary critic Louise Westling argues that “Hulga's adoption of nihilism functions as a defense mechanism rather than a genuine philosophical position” (48).

Single number in the parenthetical. Period after the closing parenthesis.

Section Four

Pattern C: HTML Article, Author's Name in the Citation

When your secondary source is an HTML article (no fixed page numbers) and you have not named the author in the signal phrase, include only the author's last name in the parenthetical citation. There is no page number. Do not invent or estimate one.

Pattern C Format: “quotation” (LastName).
✗ Invented page number

Scholars of O'Connor have observed that the mother daughter relationship “functions as a stalemate between two equally limited ways of seeing” (Asals 7).

HTML articles have no fixed pages. Do not estimate, count, or invent a page number.

✓ Correct: author only

Scholars of O'Connor have observed that the mother daughter relationship “functions as a stalemate between two equally limited ways of seeing” (Asals).

Last name alone in the parenthetical. No page number.

Section Five

Pattern D: HTML Article, Author's Name in the Signal Phrase

When your secondary source is an HTML article and you have named the author in the signal phrase, no parenthetical citation is needed at all. The reader can find the full source on the Works Cited page under the author's name. End the sentence with a period after the closing quotation mark.

Pattern D Format: Author argues that “quotation.”
✗ Unnecessary citation added

O'Connor scholar Frederick Asals has observed that the mother daughter relationship “functions as a stalemate between two equally limited ways of seeing” (Asals).

Author already named in signal phrase, so the parenthetical citation is redundant. Remove it.

✓ Correct: no parenthetical

O'Connor scholar Frederick Asals has observed that the mother daughter relationship “functions as a stalemate between two equally limited ways of seeing.”

No citation. Period goes inside the closing quotation mark, since this is the one situation where no parenthetical follows.

A Note on Final Punctuation

When there is no parenthetical citation, American English convention places the period inside the closing quotation mark: “quotation.” This is the one situation where a period appears inside the quotation marks. When a parenthetical citation follows, the period always goes after the closing parenthesis: “quotation” (Author Page).

Section Six

Articles With No Identified Author

Occasionally, an article in an academic database will not list a named author. In that case, use the first one to three significant words of the article's title inside the parenthetical citation, in quotation marks. These sources are relatively rare in ENC 1102, and it is generally preferable to choose sources with identifiable authors when possible. Named scholars carry more scholarly weight. If your source is a PDF and has a page number, include the page number after the title fragment.

No Author Format: “quotation” (“First Words”) for HTML, or (“First Words” PageNumber) for PDF
✓ HTML source, no author

One overview of O'Connor's fiction notes that her characters consistently “experience moments of violent grace that strip away comfortable illusions” (“Overview of O'Connor”).

No author identified, so the first significant words of the title appear in quotation marks. HTML article: no page number.

✓ PDF source, no author (page included)

Critics have noted that O'Connor uses physical disability “as an outward sign of a character's spiritual condition and resistance to grace” (“Disability in O'Connor” 38).

PDF article with no author: title fragment in quotation marks, followed by the page number.

The Most Common Error

By far the most common error is repeating the author's name in both the signal phrase and the citation. Pick one location for the name and use it there only. See the “Author Named Twice” section below.

Part Two Common Quoting Errors

Common Error

Floating Quotes

A floating quote is a quotation that stands alone as its own sentence without being introduced by a signal phrase or integrated into your own prose. Every quotation from a secondary source must be attached to your own words, both before and after: a signal phrase establishes who is speaking and why their words matter, and your own analysis after the quote explains what the evidence contributes to your argument.

✗ Floating quote

Hulga's nihilism protects her from genuine feeling. “Hulga's adoption of nihilism functions as a defense mechanism rather than a genuine philosophical position” (Westling 48). O'Connor uses this to show her flaws.

The quote stands alone as its own sentence with no signal phrase introducing it and only a vague sentence following it.

✓ Quote introduced and analyzed

Literary critic Louise Westling argues that “Hulga's adoption of nihilism functions as a defense mechanism rather than a genuine philosophical position, a way of preempting rejection by rejecting everything first” (48). Westling's reading clarifies what the story's opening pages establish: Hulga's nihilism is not the conclusion of rigorous reasoning but the emotional response of someone who found the world painful.

Signal phrase, quoted material, and follow up analysis all in place.

The Signal, Quote, Analysis Pattern

Every secondary source use should follow the same three part structure: (1) a signal phrase that names the scholar and establishes context, (2) the quoted or paraphrased material, and (3) your own sentence or two of analysis connecting the scholar's point to your argument. The analysis is not optional. A quote dropped in without your interpretation does not advance your thesis.

Common Error

Comma Splices Involving Quotes

A common quoting error is using a comma to join your own complete independent clause to a quotation. If your lead in is a complete sentence, use a colon, not a comma. Alternatively, restructure the lead in so the quotation flows naturally from an incomplete construction and no colon is needed at all. The second option is usually more elegant.

✗ Comma between complete clause and quote

Westling makes an important point about this scene, “the violation of Hulga's prosthesis enacts O'Connor's characteristic use of violent grace” (54).

“Westling makes an important point” is a complete sentence. A comma cannot join it to the quotation.

✓ Colon after a complete lead in

Westling makes an important point about this scene: “the violation of Hulga's prosthesis enacts O'Connor's characteristic use of violent grace” (54).

A colon can introduce the quotation when the lead in is a complete sentence on its own.

✓ Restructured: incomplete lead in

Westling connects this moment to O'Connor's broader theological concerns, arguing that “the violation of Hulga's prosthesis enacts O'Connor's characteristic use of violent grace” (54).

“arguing that” is incomplete on its own. The quote flows naturally from it. No colon needed.

✓ The most natural form

Louise Westling argues that “the violation of Hulga's prosthesis enacts O'Connor's characteristic use of violent grace” (54).

Author named in the lead in, incomplete construction, page number only in the citation. Pattern B.

Common Error

Colon Before a Quote: Lead In Must Be a Complete Sentence

A colon may introduce a quotation only when the signal phrase is a complete sentence (subject and predicate, capable of standing alone). If your lead in is an incomplete construction such as “Westling notes that” or “according to Kaminski,” do not use a colon. Let the quotation flow directly from your words instead.

✗ Colon after incomplete lead in

Kaminski notes that: “the refusal of the call was the stage Lucas worked hardest to dramatize authentically” (172).

“Kaminski notes that” is not a complete sentence. A colon cannot follow it.

✓ No colon, quote flows naturally

Kaminski notes that “the refusal of the call was the stage Lucas worked hardest to dramatize authentically” (172).

Incomplete lead in, no colon needed.

✓ Colon correct after complete sentence

Kaminski identifies the key dramatic challenge Lucas faced: “the refusal of the call was the stage Lucas worked hardest to dramatize authentically” (172).

“Kaminski identifies the key dramatic challenge Lucas faced” is a complete sentence on its own.

✓ Same idea, no colon at all

For Kaminski, the dramatic challenge Lucas faced was clear, since “the refusal of the call was the stage Lucas worked hardest to dramatize authentically” (172).

Restructured so the quote flows from a connector. Often the most elegant move.

Common Error

Punctuation Left Inside the Quotation Marks

When a quotation is followed by a parenthetical citation, do not leave the original text's ending punctuation inside the closing quotation mark. Periods, commas, semicolons, and colons from the original text should be dropped before the closing quotation mark. The citation and your own closing punctuation handle the end of the sentence. The exceptions are question marks and exclamation marks that belong to the quoted material itself; those stay inside.

✗ Original period left inside

Westling argues that “the violation of Hulga's prosthesis enacts O'Connor's characteristic use of violent grace.” (54).

The period from the original text should not appear inside the quotation marks. It also creates double punctuation with the period after the citation.

✓ Original period removed

Westling argues that “the violation of Hulga's prosthesis enacts O'Connor's characteristic use of violent grace” (54).

Clean closing quotation mark, citation, then a single period.

✓ Exception: question mark stays inside

Gentry poses a central question about the story: “Does Hulga's nihilism differ in any meaningful way from Pointer's?” (113).

Question marks from the original text stay inside the quotation marks. The period after the citation still closes your sentence.

✓ Same rule for exclamation marks

The Misfit's flat declaration carries the weight of the story's theology: “No pleasure but meanness!” (O'Connor 5).

If an exclamation mark is part of the original quoted material, keep it inside.

Common Error

Author's Name Appearing in Both Signal Phrase and Citation

The author's name belongs in one place only: either in the signal phrase or in the parenthetical citation. Never both. When the name appears in the signal phrase, the citation contains only the page number (for PDF sources) or nothing (for HTML sources). When the name appears in the citation, the signal phrase introduces the quote without naming the author. Repeating the name in both places is redundant and counts as a citation error.

✗ Name repeated in both places

Literary critic Louise Westling argues that “the violation of Hulga's prosthesis enacts O'Connor's characteristic use of violent grace” (Westling 54).

Author named in signal phrase, so remove the name from the citation. Page number only: (54).

✓ Name in signal phrase only

Literary critic Louise Westling argues that “the violation of Hulga's prosthesis enacts O'Connor's characteristic use of violent grace” (54).

Pattern B in action. Single page number in the citation.

✓ Name in citation only

One critic connects this moment to O'Connor's theological vision, arguing that “the violation of Hulga's prosthesis enacts O'Connor's characteristic use of violent grace” (Westling 54).

No name in signal phrase, so the last name appears in the citation with the page number. Pattern A.

✓ HTML version of the same rule

One critic argues that O'Connor's mother daughter scenes function as “a stalemate between two equally limited ways of seeing” (Asals).

HTML source, no name in signal phrase, so the last name alone appears in the citation. Pattern C.

Common Error

Missing End Punctuation After a Quote

When a quotation ends your sentence, the sentence still needs its own closing punctuation, a period (or semicolon, or colon), placed after the parenthetical citation. Students frequently omit this punctuation entirely. The period after the citation is not optional. When there is no parenthetical citation (HTML source, author in signal phrase), the period goes inside the closing quotation mark instead.

✗ Missing period after citation

Kaminski notes that the act of switching off the targeting computer was designed to be “the single most important gesture in the film, the moment Luke stops performing heroism and actually becomes a hero” (198)

The sentence ends after the closing parenthesis with no period.

✓ Period after citation

Kaminski notes that the act of switching off the targeting computer was designed to be “the single most important gesture in the film, the moment Luke stops performing heroism and actually becomes a hero” (198).

Citation, then a single period closing the sentence.

✓ Semicolon after citation: joining clauses

Gentry argues that both characters carry “systems of belief emptied of genuine content and filled with something self serving” (113); this framing explains why their encounter is a collision rather than a seduction.

A semicolon after the citation is correct when joining two independent clauses.

✓ HTML source: period inside the quotes

O'Connor scholar Frederick Asals has observed that the mother daughter conflict “refuses to endorse either as a reliable window onto reality.”

No parenthetical citation, so the period goes inside the closing quotation mark.

Quick Self Check Before You Submit

For every secondary source quotation in your draft, check three things: (1) the format of the source (PDF or HTML) determines whether you need a page number, (2) the author's name appears once, in either the signal phrase or the citation but not both, and (3) the sentence ends with one piece of closing punctuation, in the right place for the citation pattern you used. Most secondary source citation problems are caught by those three quick checks.
