Vladimir Nabokov Владимир Владимирович Набоков

1899 — 1977

⌂ St. Petersburg → → → ◈ Ithaca, NY

An aristocrat who lost everything in a revolution, a Russian who became an English master, a butterfly hunter who became a Harvard scientist, a European exile who reinvented himself in a succession of hotel rooms and rented houses — and who described memory, language, and consciousness with a precision no one has matched before or since.

47 Morskaya Street — the world before the revolution

Vladimir Nabokov was born in 1899 into one of the wealthiest and most distinguished families in Russia. His father, Vladimir Dmitrievich Nabokov, was a prominent liberal statesman who had been imprisoned by the Tsar and would later be assassinated by a right-wing gunman in Berlin — throwing himself in front of a colleague to absorb the bullet. The family lived in a large mansion on Morskaya Street in St. Petersburg, kept three country estates, spoke English and French at home as naturally as Russian, and possessed a library of extraordinary depth.

The childhood Nabokov describes in his autobiography Speak, Memory (1951) is one of the most dazzling accounts of privileged youth in all of literature — and also one of the most elegiac. He knew, writing it in American exile, that the world he was describing had been destroyed when he was nineteen. The Revolution of 1917 ended everything. The family fled to England, then to Berlin. Nabokov would not see Russia again for the rest of his life.

Vyra · The Summer Estate

It is not St. Petersburg but the family's summer estate at Vyra — birch forests, meadows, the stream, the particular quality of Russian summer light — that haunts Nabokov's writing most persistently. He caught his first butterfly there. He fell in love there. He spent every summer of his childhood there. In Speak, Memory he reconstructs it with such hallucinatory precision that readers sometimes feel they have been there themselves. The estate was destroyed during the Second World War. Nabokov never went back.

Nabokov hunting butterflies · American West · c. 1954

Nabokov was a serious scientist. Not a gentleman naturalist dabbling in butterflies — a genuine research lepidopterist whose monograph on the Polyommatus blues of the New World, completed at Harvard's Museum of Natural History in the 1940s, was later vindicated by DNA analysis in 2011. He had predicted, from morphological evidence alone, that the blues he was studying had migrated from Asia to the Americas in five separate waves over millions of years. The genetics confirmed it exactly. He spent some of his most productive literary years bent over a microscope in a Harvard basement, and he considered the two vocations — literature and science — to be expressions of the same impulse: the drive to see precisely.

"The highest concentration of human happiness," he said, "is found in the hunting of butterflies." He drove across America each summer — a large American car, Nabokov at the wheel, his wife Véra navigating, the net in the back seat — hunting specimens in the mountain meadows of Colorado and Arizona. His collection is at the Harvard Museum of Natural History. The Karner blue butterfly, Lycaeides melissa samuelis, which he formally described and named, is now an endangered species. It bears his analysis in the scientific literature forever.

The Specimen and the Story

Nabokov described his method in fiction the same way he described it in science: total, obsessive observation of the individual specimen, followed by the discovery of a pattern so precise it verges on the miraculous. He disliked allegory, symbolism, and "messages." He believed that the truth was in the surface — in the exact color of a wing, the exact quality of a memory, the exact weight of a word. "Caress the details," he told his Cornell students. "The divine details."

Nabokov at the Cornell lectern · c. 1954

In 1948, Nabokov became Professor of Russian Literature at Cornell University in Ithaca, New York — a position he would hold until 1959, when the success of Lolita made him wealthy enough to retire. The eleven Cornell years were among his most productive: Lolita, Pnin, the Lectures on Literature, and the translation of Pushkin's Eugene Onegin were all Ithaca products. He lived in a series of rented houses — he and Véra never owned property in America — and spent his summers driving the American West in search of butterfly specimens.

His teaching was legendary. He prepared every lecture as meticulously as a short story, read from a written text, tolerated no interruptions, and demanded that students attend to the physical text with the same precision he brought to a butterfly specimen. "Fondle the details," he told them. He drew floor plans of fictional houses on the blackboard. He made students describe the exact color of Madame Bovary's eyes. He was, by all accounts, impossible and unforgettable.

8 Craigie Circle, Cambridge, MA

"Signs and Symbols" was written in Cambridge while Nabokov was still working at Harvard's entomology department. The Nabokovs rented at 8 Craigie Circle — a modest address in a city of academic grandeur, wholly unlike the St. Petersburg mansion. The story's setting — an unnamed American city in winter, a bus ride, a hospital that cannot be reached, an apartment where the telephone will not stop ringing — carries the quality of Cambridge in February: grey, cold, full of meaning that cannot quite be deciphered.

Ithaca, NY · February — the world of the story

"Signs and Symbols" (1948) is three pages long and contains more precisely observed reality than most novels. An elderly Jewish immigrant couple in an unnamed American city travel by bus to a sanitarium to visit their son on his birthday. They are turned back — he has attempted suicide again. They return home. The mother cannot sleep. They look at old photographs. The telephone rings twice — wrong numbers. Then it rings a third time. The story ends.

Nothing is resolved. Nothing is explained. The son's condition — what Nabokov names "referential mania" — involves the belief that everything in the outside world is secretly communicating to him, that clouds, passing strangers, the patterns in wallpaper, are all part of an elaborate and sinister system of signs aimed specifically at him. He is not wrong that the world is full of patterns. He is only wrong that they are aimed at him.

Referential Mania — Nabokov's Clinical Description

In the story, Nabokov gives us the doctor's diagnosis: "the patient imagines that everything happening around him is a veiled reference to his personality and existence." Clouds in the staircase of a deformed into a message. Faces of strangers conceal a fiendish intent. Everything that can be seen, heard, or felt is a sign directed at him. The world has become a text — and he is its only subject.

The critical question the story raises is whether the parents are experiencing something analogous. Their evening is saturated with small signs and symbols — the jam jars, the photographs, the wrong-number calls — that the narrative presents with such care that the reader cannot help feeling they mean something. The final phone call, which the story refuses to complete, is the hinge: is it the hospital calling to say the son has died? Or is it another wrong number? Or — and this is Nabokov's deepest trick — is it that we, as readers, have been infected with the son's condition?

Synesthesia and Referential Mania — The Connection

Some critics have read the son's condition as a dark mirror of Nabokov's synesthesia. Both involve the perception of a world in which sensory experience is overdetermined — where letters have colors, where objects carry messages. The difference is the difference between a gift and an illness: Nabokov's colored letters were a source of pleasure and precision; the son's referential mania is a source of terror. The story is, in part, about what happens when the capacity to see patterns — to read the world as a system of meaningful signs — exceeds the human ability to bear it.

Scene One · Opening

The Bus Ride in Rain

The story opens with the couple already in motion — on a bus, on their way to the sanitarium, the birthday present on the woman's lap. Nabokov gives us their exhaustion in small precise details: the man's feet hurt, the woman's hands, the rain. They do not speak. They have been married for decades; the silence is not hostility but the particular compression of two people who share a grief too large for words.

Scene Two · The Barrier

Turned Back at the Entrance

They arrive and are told the visit is impossible — the son has attempted suicide again. They must leave. The man still holds the birthday gift. The glass doors of the sanitarium are the story's central image of the uncrossable threshold: the son is in there, they are out here, and the glass that separates them is as absolute as death. Hopper painted this exact situation — the human figure visible through glass, separated from warmth or connection by a transparent barrier that might as well be a wall.

Scene Three · The Charged Object

The Photographs at Midnight

Unable to sleep, the mother takes out a box of old photographs. Nabokov lists what she sees: the child at various ages, her late mother-in-law, a dog that died before the war. The jam jars, labeled in her handwriting, sit on the shelf. The telephone is on the wall. Everything in this kitchen is saturated with time and loss — and the reader, infected by now with the son's referential mania, cannot help feeling that the jam jars and the photographs and the telephone are all signs in a system. What are they signs of?

Scene Four — The Last Image

The Telephone Rings a Third Time

The story's final sentence: the phone rings a third time. The story ends. Nabokov does not tell us who is calling. Critics have argued ever since. The two most likely readings — it is the hospital calling to say the son has died, or it is another wrong number — are both plausible and both terrible. The genius of the ending is that both readings are simultaneously true: in a story about a world saturated with portentous meaning, the final sign is the one we cannot decode. The reader is left holding the receiver.

Feeling: suspended time, the moment before. Hopper's pregnant ordinary moment. No horror — the terrible weight of the ordinary. Painterly, slow, precise.

§ 05

What to Carry Into the Reading

Nabokov said he had no interest in allegory, symbol, or "message." He was lying, or at least being provocative. "Signs and Symbols" is a story about whether the world means anything — and it is written by a man who had lost a paradise that no longer existed, who saw colors in letters, who pinned butterflies with the same care with which he placed words, and who believed that the precision of observation was the only dignity available to a conscious being in a universe that did not explain itself.

The Three Questions the Story Leaves Open

First: who is calling at the end? This is the surface question, and it matters — but it is not the deepest question.

Second: is the parents' world also a world of signs and symbols? Are the jam jars and the photographs and the wrong-number calls meaningful, or does the story only make them seem so? Is the reader experiencing referential mania by the final page?

Third, and deepest: if everything is a sign, and if the signs all point somewhere, where do they point? Nabokov refused to answer. The refusal is the answer.

The story says: look closely. Look at the jam jars. Look at the photographs. Count the phone calls. And then ask yourself whether what you are doing — looking closely, counting, finding patterns — is the activity of a careful reader, or the first symptom of the son's condition. — Not Nabokov · a description of what the story does to you
