Module Seven  ·  MLA Documentation

How to Create Your Works Cited

A step-by-step walkthrough for building, formatting, and finalizing your Works Cited page.

Before You Begin

Have the following open and ready before you start working through the eight steps below:

  • Your completed Poetry Research Essay Source Analysis Assignment — your three secondary source MLA citations are already there
  • Your research essay document, open and ready to edit
  • The Works Cited Entries for Your Poem page — linked on the previous page; you will need it for Step 3
Follow Along with Jordan Rivera

Jordan is writing a poetry research essay about Sylvia Plath's "Morning Song." She has already completed her Source Analysis Assignment and has three secondary source citations ready to go — the same three sources used in the sample essay on the previous module page. At each step, a Jordan's Example section shows exactly what her Works Cited looks like at that stage. Follow along to see how a real Works Cited page comes together from start to finish.

1

Copy Your Secondary Source Citations

Open your completed Poetry Research Essay Source Analysis Assignment. Your three MLA citations are listed there, each under its own source section. For each source, select and copy the MLA Citation and then paste it into your research essay document to begin building your Works Cited. Paste all three entries one after another — you will alphabetize them in Step 2.

Tip: Paste the entries at the very end of your essay document, after your conclusion paragraph. You may want to insert a page break first so the Works Cited starts on its own page.

2

Alphabetize Your Entries

You now have three secondary source citations in your document. Arrange them in alphabetical order by the author's last name. If an entry has no author, alphabetize it by the first significant word of the article title.

Note: If the first word of an authorless title is "A," "An," or "The," skip it and alphabetize by the next word instead.

3

Add Your Primary Source Entry

Your primary source — the poem you wrote about — also needs a Works Cited entry. Because you're special, that entry is already formatted for you.

Follow these four moves to obtain and place your primary source Works Cited entry:

1.   Open the Works Cited Entries for Your Poem page (linked from the previous module page).
2.   Locate the pre-formatted Works Cited entry for your chosen poem.
3.   Copy it and paste it into your Works Cited alongside your three secondary source entries.
4.   Place it in its correct alphabetical position by the poet's last name.

Note: After pasting, check whether the entry came through with a URL styled as a blue hyperlink. If so, you will fix that in Step 4.

4

Fix URL Formatting

When you paste entries from a database or webpage, URLs often arrive with two separate problems. Check for both:

Problem: Empty gaps appearing before or within a URL. Long URLs sometimes create an awkward blank space on the line just before them. Fix: See Video 2 on the MLA Documentation: The Works Cited page for the technique.

Problem: URL appears as blue underlined hyperlink text. MLA requires URLs as plain black text, not clickable links. Fix: Right-click the hyperlink and select Remove Hyperlink. Then confirm the text color matches the rest of the entry.

5

Apply Hanging Indents

Every Works Cited entry requires a hanging indent: the first line of the entry sits at the left margin, and every line after it is indented half an inch.

Manual Method

Place your cursor at the beginning of each continuation line and press Tab once to indent it.

Automatic Method (recommended)

Use your word processor's Hanging Indent paragraph formatting option. Video 1 on the previous page demonstrates this step by step.

Note: Applying indents can shift URL spacing again. After this step, go back and double-check Step 4 to make sure URL gaps and hyperlinks are still corrected.

Jordan's Example

6

Adjust Spacing

The Works Cited page must be double-spaced throughout — both within each entry and between entries. Select all the text on the Works Cited page and apply double spacing.

Common mistake: Extra blank lines between entries. Do not insert a blank line between entries to create visual separation. The space between entries should look identical to the space between lines within a single entry. Fix: Delete any blank lines between entries, then select all and apply double spacing in a single pass.

Jordan's Example

Jordan had pressed Enter twice between entries to add visual space. She deletes the extra blank lines, then selects all four entries and sets line spacing to double. The result is consistent spacing throughout.

7

Italicize Journal, Database, and Website Titles

When you paste bibliographic information from a database or website, the original italics are often stripped. Go through each entry carefully and italicize any of the following types of titles that appear:

Journal and magazine titles
e.g., Oswald Review, The New Inquiry
Database titles
e.g., EBSCOhost, Gale Literature: LitFinder
Anthology and website titles
e.g., The Norton Anthology of Poetry, Poetry Foundation

Reminder: Article titles, poem titles, and short story titles go in quotation marks, not italics. Only the containing publication, anthology, database, or website name is italicized.

8

Add the Works Cited Heading

At the very top of the page, type the words Works Cited. Then apply the following formatting to the heading:

✓ Centered
Align it to the center of the page
✗ Not bold
Same weight as the entry text
✗ Not underlined
Plain text only
✗ Not in quotes
Write it without quotation marks

Final Checklist — Before You Submit
Titled Works Cited — centered, not bolded, not in quotes
Entries in alphabetical order by author's last name
Entire page is double-spaced — no extra blank lines
Hanging indent applied to all entries
All URLs are plain black text — no blue hyperlinks
Font and size match the rest of the essay
Four entries total: one primary source + three secondary sources
Every source cited in the essay appears on the Works Cited — and vice versa
