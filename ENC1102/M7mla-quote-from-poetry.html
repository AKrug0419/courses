MLA Documentation · Module Seven

How to Quote
from Poetry

The MLA 9 rules for quoting verse: signal phrases, line numbers, the slash convention for line breaks, and the most common errors to avoid.

Quoting from a poem is a little different from quoting from a short story. Poems are written in lines, and those lines are part of the poem's meaning. MLA 9 has a small set of conventions designed to preserve a poem's line structure inside your prose. This page walks you through those conventions, organized by how many lines you are quoting at a time, and then closes with the common quoting errors to watch for in your draft.

Important: Three Lines Maximum

Your poetry research essay is short. Do not quote more than three successive lines of poetry in any single quotation. Longer quotations crowd out your own analysis and turn the essay into a transcription. If a passage is longer than three lines but you only need part of it, quote just the part you need. If you need to discuss a longer passage, paraphrase it instead and quote a single phrase or short line that does the heaviest lifting.

Part One Core Skills

Section One

Signal Phrases for Poetry

A signal phrase introduces a quotation by naming the poet or providing context before the quoted words appear. For poetry, strong signal phrases identify the poet by name and often hint at what the quoted lines are doing. Avoid naming the poet in every signal phrase; doing so becomes repetitive. Instead, alternate between signal phrases that include the poet's name and signal phrases that do not, placing the poet's last name inside the parenthetical citation when it is omitted from the lead in.

Signal verbs for poetry, vary these throughout your essay: writes, describes, observes, notes, suggests, reveals, captures, figures, imagines, asks, declares, insists, frames, recasts, compares, dismisses.

Present Tense, Always

Use present tense signal verbs when writing about poetry: "Limón writes," not "Limón wrote." A poem's actions are happening every time the poem is read, even if the poet published it decades ago.

Two correct patterns, alternate between them

✓ Poet named in signal phrase

Ada Limón captures spring's loud excess in a single phrase when she calls the showy blossoms “the world's baubles and trinkets” (line 7).

Poet named in the signal phrase, so the citation contains only the line number.

✓ Poet named in citation instead

The poem's central image figures recovery as a bodily process: leaves arrive as “a green skin” that grows over winter's damage (Limón, line 9).

No poet name in the signal phrase, so the citation contains the last name and the line number.

Section Two

Line Numbers, Not Page Numbers

When you quote from a poem in MLA 9, the parenthetical citation uses line numbers, not page numbers. The first time you cite the poem, write the word "line" or "lines" before the number so the reader knows what those numbers refer to. After that first citation, you can drop the word and use the numbers alone for the rest of the essay.

Format: “quotation” (line 7) → first citation in the essay
Format: “quotation” (7) → all later citations

If the poet is not named in your signal phrase, the last name comes first inside the parentheses, separated from the line numbers by a comma: (Hayes, line 4) or, after the first citation, (Hayes 4).

✗ Page number used by mistake

Hayes describes the search engine's product as a person flattened into “bits of pixels & data” (Hayes, p. 4).

Poetry citations use line numbers, not page numbers. Never write “p.” or “page.”

✓ Line number, correct format

Hayes describes the search engine's product as a person flattened into “bits of pixels & data” (line 4).

Poet named in signal phrase, so the line number stands alone inside the parentheses.

Section Three

The Poet's Name Belongs in One Place Only

The poet's last name appears either in the signal phrase or in the parenthetical citation. Never both. Repeating the name in both places is redundant and counts as a citation error in MLA. When the name appears in the signal phrase, the parenthetical contains only the line number. When the name appears in the parenthetical, the signal phrase introduces the quotation without using the poet's name.

✗ Name repeated in both places

Limón suggests that the leaves themselves are a kind of healing when she describes them as “a green skin” (Limón, line 9).

“Limón” is already in the signal phrase. Remove it from the citation: (line 9).

✓ Name in signal phrase only

Limón suggests that the leaves themselves are a kind of healing when she describes them as “a green skin” (line 9).

The poet is named once. The citation contains only the line number.

✗ Name repeated in both places

The Hayes poem reduces a person to digital fragments, calling the search result “bits of pixels & data” (Hayes, line 4).

“Hayes” appears in both the signal phrase and the citation. Pick one location.

✓ Name in citation only

The poem reduces a person to digital fragments, calling the search result “bits of pixels & data” (Hayes, line 4).

No name in the signal phrase, so the last name belongs inside the parentheses with the line number.

Section Four

Quoting a Single Line of a Poem

When the words you want to quote sit entirely within a single line of verse, the quotation works almost exactly like a quotation from prose. Enclose the words in double quotation marks, end with a parenthetical citation that gives the line number, and place the period of your sentence after the closing parenthesis.

Format: “quoted words” (line N).
Or, after the first citation: “quoted words” (N).

Examples from Limón's “Instructions on Not Giving Up”

✓ Single line, poet in signal phrase

Limón dismisses the showy blossoms of early spring as “the world's baubles and trinkets” (line 7).

Quoted words come from a single line of verse, so no slash is needed. Poet named in the signal phrase, so the citation contains only the line number.

✓ Single line, poet in citation

The poem treats spring's color as theatrical excess, calling the bright petals “the world's baubles and trinkets” (Limón, line 7).

When the poet is not named in the lead in, the last name appears in the citation with a comma before the line number.

Example from Hayes's “American Sonnet for My Past and Future Assassin”

✓ Single line, poet in signal phrase

Hayes captures the search engine's flattening effect in a single image when he describes a person appearing as “bits of pixels & data” (line 4).

Poet named in the signal phrase. Single line of verse. Line number alone in the citation.

Section Five

Quoting Two Successive Lines of a Poem

When your quotation spans two lines of verse, you have to show the line break inside your prose. MLA 9 marks the line break with a forward slash, and a single space appears on each side of the slash. The quotation marks still go around the entire passage, and the parenthetical citation now uses a line range rather than a single number.

Format: “first line / second line” (lines N1-N2).
Note: one space before the slash, one space after. The slash signals where the poem's line broke on the page.

Examples from Limón's “Instructions on Not Giving Up”

✓ Two lines with one slash, poet in signal phrase

Limón figures the slow recovery of the trees as a bodily process when she describes the new leaves as “a green skin / growing over whatever winter did to us” (lines 9-10).

The slash with a space on either side marks the line break between lines 9 and 10. The line range goes inside the citation.

✓ Two lines with one slash, poet in citation

The poem's central image locates resilience inside the body itself: a return to “continuous living / despite the mess of us” (Limón, lines 11-12).

Poet not named in the lead in, so the last name appears in the parenthetical with a comma before the line range.

Example from Hayes's “American Sonnet for My Past and Future Assassin”

✓ Two lines with one slash, poet in signal phrase

Hayes builds the poem's central pun around the doubled word “arms,” pointing “towards the arms / Of the last trill trees of Bluff Estates” (lines 8-9).

One slash marks one line break. The line range identifies which two lines the quoted words occupy.

Watch the spacing

The slash always has a space on each side: “a green skin / growing over”, never “a green skin/growing over.” Word processors sometimes auto correct the spacing; check it during proofreading.

Section Six

Quoting Three Successive Lines of a Poem

When a quotation spans three successive lines of verse, the same rules apply: a forward slash marks each line break, with a space on either side. Three lines means two slashes, one for each break. The line range in the citation now spans three numbers.

Format: “first line / second line / third line” (lines N1-N3).
Three Lines is the Limit

Three successive lines is the most you should quote in a single quotation in this essay. If your draft contains a longer block of verse, cut it. Quote just the words that matter most, paraphrase the rest, and use the space you save for analysis.

Example from Limón's “Instructions on Not Giving Up”

✓ Three lines with two slashes, poet in signal phrase

Limón closes the poem with a gesture that performs its entire emotional argument: “Fine then, I'll take it, the tree seems to say, / a new slick leaf unfurling / like a fist to an open palm” (lines 12-14).

Two slashes mark two line breaks. The citation gives the full three line range.

Example from Hayes's “American Sonnet for My Past and Future Assassin”

✓ Three lines with two slashes, poet in citation

The poem connects the destruction of trees to the destruction of bodies through a single chain of images: “the slim fly girls the color of trees cut down & shaped / Into something a nail penetrates. I admit, right now / Technology is insufficient” (Hayes, lines 10-12).

Two slashes for two line breaks. Poet's last name in the parenthetical because the lead in does not name him.

✗ Slashes missing

Hayes connects the trees to the bodies in “the color of trees cut down & shaped Into something a nail penetrates. I admit, right now Technology is insufficient” (lines 10-12).

The line breaks in the original verse are not marked. Without slashes, the reader cannot see where the lines end.

✓ Slashes mark each break

Hayes connects the trees to the bodies in “the color of trees cut down & shaped / Into something a nail penetrates. I admit, right now / Technology is insufficient” (lines 10-12).

Each forward slash, with a space on each side, shows the reader where one line of verse ends and the next begins.

Part Two Common Quoting Errors

Common Error

Floating Quotes

A floating quote (also called a dropped or isolated quote) is a quotation that stands alone as its own sentence, without being introduced by a signal phrase or integrated into one of your own sentences. Every quotation must be attached to your own words, both before and after, so that the reader can see who is speaking and what the quoted words are doing in your argument.

✗ Floating quote

The poem suggests that recovery is bodily. “a green skin / growing over whatever winter did to us” (Limón, lines 9-10). This shows healing.

The quoted lines stand alone as their own sentence with no signal phrase. The sentence after the quote adds nothing.

✓ Quote integrated and analyzed

Limón locates resilience inside the body when she describes the new leaves as “a green skin / growing over whatever winter did to us” (lines 9-10). Skin heals over wounds, and by giving the tree a body, Limón turns the arrival of leaves into an act of healing rather than a season change.

The quote is introduced by a signal phrase and followed by sentences that explain what the image is doing in the poem.

Common Error

Comma Splices Involving Quotes

A common quoting error is using a comma to join your own complete sentence to a quotation. If your lead in is a complete sentence, use a colon, not a comma. A second option, often more elegant, is to restructure the lead in so the quotation flows naturally from an incomplete construction (such as “Limón writes that” or “Hayes describes”), in which case no colon is needed.

✗ Comma between complete sentence and quote

Limón turns the arrival of leaves into a quiet act of recovery, “a green skin / growing over whatever winter did to us” (lines 9-10).

“Limón turns the arrival of leaves into a quiet act of recovery” is a complete sentence. A comma cannot join it to the quotation.

✓ Colon after a complete lead in

Limón turns the arrival of leaves into a quiet act of recovery: “a green skin / growing over whatever winter did to us” (lines 9-10).

A colon can introduce a quotation when the lead in is a complete sentence on its own.

✓ Restructured: quote flows from an incomplete lead in

Limón turns the arrival of leaves into a quiet act of recovery by describing them as “a green skin / growing over whatever winter did to us” (lines 9-10).

“by describing them as” is incomplete on its own, so the quotation flows directly from it without any colon.

Common Error

Colon Before a Quote: Lead In Must Be a Complete Sentence

A colon may introduce a quotation only when the signal phrase before it is a complete sentence (subject and predicate, capable of standing alone). If the lead in is an incomplete construction such as “Hayes notes that” or “according to Limón,” do not use a colon. Let the quotation flow directly from your words instead.

✗ Colon after incomplete lead in

Hayes notes that: “bits of pixels & data” (line 4).

“Hayes notes that” is not a complete sentence. A colon cannot follow it.

✓ No colon, quote flows naturally

Hayes notes that the search engine reduces a person to “bits of pixels & data” (line 4).

“Hayes notes that” is incomplete, so the quotation flows directly from it. No colon needed.

✓ Colon correct after a complete sentence

Hayes identifies the central failure of the search engine: “bits of pixels & data” (line 4).

“Hayes identifies the central failure of the search engine” is a complete sentence on its own, so the colon is correct.

Common Error

Punctuation Left Inside the Quotation Marks

When a quotation is followed by a parenthetical citation, do not leave the original poem's ending punctuation inside the closing quotation mark. Periods, commas, semicolons, and colons from the poem itself should be dropped before the closing quotation mark, because the citation and your own closing punctuation handle the end of the sentence. The exceptions are question marks and exclamation marks that belong to the quoted words; those stay inside.

✗ Original punctuation left inside

Hayes flattens a person to “bits of pixels & data.” (line 4).

The period before the closing quotation mark is not from the poem and creates double punctuation with the period after the citation.

✓ Punctuation after the citation

Hayes flattens a person to “bits of pixels & data” (line 4).

No period inside the quotation mark. The period after the closing parenthesis ends the sentence.

Common Error

Missing End Punctuation After a Quote

When a quotation ends your sentence, the sentence still needs its own closing punctuation, placed after the parenthetical citation. Students frequently omit this final period. The period after the citation is not optional.

✗ Missing period after citation

Limón closes with the leaf “unfurling / like a fist to an open palm” (lines 13-14)

The sentence ends after the closing parenthesis with no period at all.

✓ Period after the citation

Limón closes with the leaf “unfurling / like a fist to an open palm” (lines 13-14).

The period after the closing parenthesis closes your sentence cleanly.

Quick Self Check Before You Submit

Before you turn in your draft, search through every quotation from a poem and check three things: (1) the citation uses line numbers, not page numbers, (2) the poet's name appears once, in either the signal phrase or the citation but not both, and (3) every line break inside the quotation is marked with a forward slash with a space on either side. Most poetry citation problems are caught by those three quick checks.
